## the directions a matrix can't change

The definition looks simple: Av = λv. A matrix times a vector equals a scalar
times that same vector. I stared at that for a while before it meant anything.
What finally made it real was drawing it.

---

### the geometric picture

Take a 2×2 matrix and feed it every point on the unit circle. What comes out is
an ellipse — the circle gets stretched and possibly rotated. Most vectors get
knocked off their original direction entirely. But the eigenvectors don't. They
sit exactly along the axes of that ellipse. The matrix can only stretch them,
not rotate them. λ is just how much.

The gray circle is the input. The purple ellipse is the output. The colored
lines are eigenvectors — they pass through the transformation unchanged in
direction. Drag the sliders or try a preset.

### the algebra, motivated by the geometry

If Av = λv, then (A − λI)v = 0. For that to have a nonzero solution,
(A − λI) has to be singular — it has to squash space down a dimension.
That only happens when its determinant is zero:

{"\\det(A - \\lambda I) = 0"}

For a 2×2 matrix [[a,b],[c,d]] this expands to:

{"\\lambda^2 - (a+d)\\lambda + (ad-bc) = 0"}

The trace (a+d) and determinant (ad−bc) fully determine the eigenvalues.

---

### when it gets interesting

Try rotation preset. The unit circle maps back to a unit
circle. No direction is preserved. The eigenvalues are ±i. There are no real
eigenvectors.

looking at the numbers — [[0,−1],[1,0]] — It gives no intuition for
why this matrix wouldn't have eigenvectors. But the geometric picture makes it obvious, almost trivial: if
the transformation is a pure rotation, there's no direction that gets left
alone. Of course there are no real eigenvectors. The geometry makes the algebra
feel inevitable instead of arbitrary.

The general principle: real eigenvectors don't exist whenever the transformation
spins space in a way that no vector lands back on its own line. This happens
any time the transformation has a rotational component strong enough that every
direction gets knocked off course — pure rotations, but also certain
combinations of rotation and reflection where the geometry never settles on a
fixed axis. Algebraically, this shows up as a negative discriminant in the
characteristic polynomial. Geometrically, it just means the ellipse is turned
too far for any input direction to survive.

---

### why care about this

I work on a market regime classification project with the UIUC Data Science Club.
PCA comes up constantly — it's how you find the directions of maximum variance
in high-dimensional data. Those directions are eigenvectors of the covariance
matrix. The eigenvalues tell you how much variance each one captures.

Understanding what eigenvalues geometrically mean makes PCA not feel like magic.
It's just finding the axes of the ellipse your data lives on.
