two vectors are orthogonal if their dot product is zero. that definition is short
enough to memorize and easy enough to use. it also hides why the idea is so powerful.

orthogonality is the geometry of not contributing to a direction.

If `u dot v = 0`, then `u` has no shadow along `v`. Moving in the `u` direction
does not move you forward or backward along `v`. That is stronger than just being
different directions. It is a clean separation of information.

---

### projecting onto a vector

Suppose you have a vector `b` and a nonzero direction `u`. The projection of `b` onto
the line spanned by `u` is the part of `b` that lies in that direction:

<div className="math-highlight">proj_u(b) = ((b dot u) / (u dot u)) u</div>

If `u` has length 1, this becomes

<div className="math-highlight">proj_u(b) = (b dot u) u</div>

The leftover piece

<div className="math-highlight">b - proj_u(b)</div>

is perpendicular to `u`. That is the whole point. Projection splits a vector into
one part along the subspace and one part orthogonal to it.

The shortest error meets the line at a right angle. If the error were not perpendicular,
you could slide a little farther along the line and make it shorter.

That one sentence is the geometric heart of least squares.

---

### projecting onto a subspace

Now replace the line with a subspace `W`. The projection of `b` onto `W` is the closest
vector in `W` to `b`.

If the columns of a matrix `A` span `W`, then every vector in `W` has the form `Ax`.
So finding the closest point in `W` means finding the `x` that makes `Ax` as close as
possible to `b`.

At the best choice, the residual is orthogonal to every column of `A`:

<div className="math-highlight">A^T(b - Ax) = 0</div>

Rearranging gives the normal equations:

<div className="math-highlight">A^T A x = A^T b</div>

This is not a formula that appears from nowhere. It is the condition "the error is
perpendicular to every direction you were allowed to use."

---

### why orthogonal bases are nice

If a basis is orthogonal, projecting becomes almost embarrassingly simple. You can
measure each coordinate with a dot product, one direction at a time, without solving
a tangled system.

That is why Gram-Schmidt exists. It takes a basis that spans the right subspace and
turns it into an orthogonal basis for the same subspace. The span stays the same, but
the coordinates become easier to read.

This also explains why SVD and Fourier series feel so clean. Both are built around
orthogonal directions, so the pieces do not interfere with one another.

---

### what this connects to

Least squares regression is projection onto the column space of the design matrix.
PCA chooses orthogonal directions that capture the most variance. SVD builds a whole
matrix out of orthogonal input and output directions.

The next missing idea is the column space itself: what outputs a matrix can actually
reach.
