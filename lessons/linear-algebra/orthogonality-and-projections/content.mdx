two vectors are orthogonal if their dot product is zero. that's the definition.
but there's a lot packed into it, and the projection that comes with it is one
of the most useful geometric ideas in all of linear algebra.

### projecting onto a vector

[TODO: given a vector b and a direction u, the projection of b onto u is the
component of b in the direction of u. formula: proj = (b·u / u·u) u.
if u is a unit vector this simplifies to (b·u)u.
the residual b - proj is orthogonal to u. this is the key fact.]

<div className="math-highlight">projᵤ(b) = (b·u / u·u) u</div>

---

### projecting onto a subspace

[TODO: the natural extension. given a subspace (spanned by some columns of a
matrix A), the projection of b onto that subspace is the closest point in the
subspace to b. closest means the residual is orthogonal to every vector in the
subspace. this gives Aᵀ(b - Ax) = 0, which rearranges to the normal equations:
AᵀAx = Aᵀb.

pause here moment: this is where least squares comes from. when Ax = b has no
solution (overdetermined system), the best you can do is minimize the residual.
the minimum is exactly the projection.]

<div className="math-highlight">AᵀAx = Aᵀb</div>
