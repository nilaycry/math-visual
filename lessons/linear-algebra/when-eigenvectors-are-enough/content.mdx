eigenvectors are the dream case. a matrix may do complicated things to most vectors,
but along an eigenvector it behaves like simple scaling:

<div className="math-highlight">Av = lambda v</div>

If you have enough eigenvectors to form a basis, the whole transformation becomes easy
to understand. Every input vector can be decomposed into eigenvector directions, and
the matrix scales each direction separately.

That is diagonalization.

---

### what diagonalization is saying

Suppose `A` has a basis of eigenvectors `v1, ..., vn`. Put those eigenvectors into
a matrix `P`. Then

<div className="math-highlight">A = P D P^{-1}</div>

where `D` is diagonal and contains the eigenvalues.

Read from right to left, this says:

- `P^{-1}` rewrites the input in eigenvector coordinates
- `D` scales each eigenvector coordinate independently
- `P` moves back to the original coordinates

So diagonalization is not just a computational trick. It means the matrix has a
coordinate system where the action separates into independent one-dimensional scalings.

---

### why this can fail

Not every matrix has enough eigenvectors.

A rotation of the plane by 90 degrees has no real eigenvectors. It moves every real
line to a different line. There is no real direction it only scales.

Some matrices have repeated eigenvalues but too few independent eigenvectors. These
are defective. They have invariant directions, but not enough to make a coordinate
system out of them.

That is the limitation of eigen-analysis: it only explains the matrix through
directions that the matrix sends back onto themselves.

---

### the symmetric miracle

Symmetric matrices are special. If `A = A^T`, then the spectral theorem says `A` has
an orthonormal basis of eigenvectors.

That is why symmetric matrices are so pleasant. Their eigenvectors are not merely
available; they are perpendicular and complete. The matrix really does become
"rotate into a good basis, scale independently, rotate back."

This is also why `A^T A` shows up in SVD. No matter what `A` is, the matrix `A^T A`
is symmetric and positive semidefinite. It has the eigenvectors we wish `A` itself
always had.

---

### why SVD takes over

SVD changes the question.

Eigenvectors ask for input directions that come back along the same line after applying
`A`. Singular vectors ask for input directions that become perpendicular output
directions after applying $A$.

That weaker request always succeeds.

So eigenvectors are enough when the matrix has a full eigenbasis, especially in the
symmetric case. SVD is the more durable story when the matrix rotates too much, loses
dimensions, is rectangular, or simply refuses to be diagonalized.
