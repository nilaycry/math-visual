the definition is really simple: Av = λv. a matrix times a vector equals a scalar
times that same vector. it seems arbitrary. why would this particular equation matter?

---

### the geometric picture

take a 2×2 matrix and feed it every point on the unit circle. the output is an ellipse. most vectors get
knocked off their original direction entirely. but the eigenvectors don't. they
sit exactly along the axes of that ellipse. the matrix can only stretch them,
not rotate them. λ is just how much.

the gray circle is the input. the purple ellipse is the output. the colored
lines are eigenvectors: they pass through the transformation unchanged in
direction. click anywhere on the unit circle to place a vector and watch where the matrix sends it. when you land on an eigenvector, the output arrow points in exactly the same direction as the input, stretched or shrunk but not rotated. any other starting point, the output swings to a different direction entirely.

### the algebra, motivated by the geometry

if Av = λv, then (A − λI)v = 0. for that to have a nonzero solution,
(A − λI) has to be singular, squash space down a dimension.
that only happens when its determinant is zero:

<div className="math-highlight">det(A − λI) = 0</div>

for a 2×2 matrix [[a, b], [c, d]] this expands to:

<div className="math-highlight">λ² − (a+d)λ + (ad−bc) = 0</div>

the trace (a+d) and determinant (ad−bc) fully determine the eigenvalues. and since this is a quadratic with λ₁ and λ₂ as roots, Vieta's formulas give you something worth remembering:

<div className="math-highlight">λ₁ + λ₂ = trace(A) &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; λ₁ · λ₂ = det(A)</div>

try the Scale preset and check it yourself. it's one of those things that feels like a coincidence the first time and obvious the second.

---

### when it gets interesting

try rotation. the unit circle maps back to a unit circle. no direction is preserved. there are no real eigenvectors.

looking at the numbers [[0, −1], [1, 0]] gives no intuition for why. but the geometric picture makes it obvious, almost trivial: if the transformation is a pure rotation, there's no direction that gets left alone. of course there are no real eigenvectors. the geometry makes the algebra feel inevitable instead of arbitrary.

the discriminant of the characteristic polynomial tells you when this happens: when (a+d)² − 4(ad−bc) < 0, the eigenvalues are complex. geometrically, the transformation spins space too much for any real direction to survive.

---

### why it matters

the one I find most surprising is PageRank. Google's original ranking algorithm
is the dominant eigenvector of a giant matrix where entry (i, j) encodes how
often page j links to page i. the page you're most likely to land on after
following random links forever is the direction the web graph can't change.
it's the same equation.

quantum mechanics runs on the same structure. observable quantities correspond
to operators, and the only values you can actually measure are eigenvalues.
the state of a system before measurement is a superposition of eigenvectors.

in dynamical systems, the sign of the eigenvalues tells you whether a fixed
point is stable. positive eigenvalue: nearby trajectories run away. negative:
they come back. the geometry of the long-term behavior is entirely determined
by the spectrum.
