switching to ReLU solved the local derivative problem. gradients no longer vanish at every saturated neuron. but deep networks kept failing to train in a different way: the activations themselves would drift into bad ranges, and the gradients would either explode or become erratic. adding more layers made things worse, not better.

the three fixes that actually made deep networks work (batch normalization, residual connections, and careful initialization) didn't come from theory. they came from years of empirical work on exactly this failure mode. I think that's worth saying directly, because in retrospect they look obvious in a way they weren't.

---

### the drift problem: batch normalization

in a deep network, layer 50 depends on the outputs of layer 49. as training updates layer 49's weights, its output distribution shifts. layer 50 is perpetually chasing a moving target.

worse: if any layer outputs large values, the next activation gets pushed into saturation, and the gradient through that layer shrinks toward zero. you're back to the vanishing gradient problem, but through distribution drift rather than bad initialization.

batch normalization interrupts this after each linear layer. it takes the outputs for the current batch, computes their mean and variance, and rescales them to zero mean and unit variance:

<div className="math-highlight">x̂ = (x − μ) / √(σ² + ε)</div>

this keeps activations centered in the regime where derivatives flow cleanly, regardless of what the previous layer's weights are doing. two learned parameters γ and β are added back after normalization. they let the network shift and scale the distribution if the optimization needs it, but the default is stable.

---

### the depth problem: residual connections

even with stable activations, something unexpected happened when scaling to 100+ layers: deeper networks performed worse on training data than shallower ones. not on test data, on training data. the optimizer just couldn't find a good solution.

this is strange. a 100-layer network contains a 20-layer network as a special case: set the extra 80 layers to act as identities (`f(x) = x`) and you've recovered the shallow network. but gradient descent consistently failed to find that solution. learning "do nothing" across 80 layers of matrices and activations from random initialization turns out to be genuinely hard.

the fix reframes the problem. instead of asking each block to learn the full transformation, add a direct path that carries the input around the block:

<div className="math-highlight">y = f(x) + x</div>

now `f(x)` only needs to learn the residual: the correction to the input, not the full mapping. if the right answer is to do nothing, the optimizer just drives `f(x)` toward zero, which is easy. `y = 0 + x` gives the identity for free.

the skip path also gives the gradient a direct route backward that bypasses any block that might constrict it. this is what made hundred-layer networks trainable.

watch the gradient magnitudes at each layer. with no skip connections, they decay with depth. add them, and the gradient at early layers stays close to what it was at the output. the skip path is doing real work.

---

### the scale problem: initialization

batch normalization stabilizes training once it starts. residual connections keep gradients flowing. but both of these can be undermined before the first update if the initial weights are badly scaled.

weights that are too large push activations into saturation on the very first forward pass. weights that are too small collapse the signal to near zero before the first backward pass. either way, gradients are useless before training begins.

the solution is to bound the initial variance so a layer's output distribution has roughly the same scale as its input. Kaiming initialization does this for ReLU networks: weights drawn from a distribution with variance `2/n`, where `n` is the number of inputs to that layer. the factor of 2 accounts for ReLU zeroing out half the activations.

<div className="math-highlight">W ~ N(0, √(2/n))</div>

this isn't theoretically derived from first principles. it's the empirically validated answer to "what scale keeps forward and backward signals alive across many layers?" there are variations for other activations, but the goal is always the same: keep the arithmetic level across depth.

---

### what this doesn't cover yet

these three fixes stabilize optimization. they don't say anything about whether the model is actually learning the right thing. generalization, overfitting, and regularization are a separate story. the next set of lessons picks up there.
