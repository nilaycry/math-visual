before any discussion of neural networks or gradients, learning is the geometry of fitting a shape to points.

this lesson is about what that "fitting" actually means.

---

### the model

suppose you have a cloud of points in space. there's a visible trend: as you move right along the first axis, the points drift identically along the second. you want a rule that captures that geometry so you can predict where the next point will land.

the simplest rule is a line: `y = w · x + b`. 

here, `x` is the input and `y` is the prediction. the constants `w` (weight) and `b` (bias) are the **parameters**. they define the line completely. pick a `w` and a `b`, and you get a specific line. change them, and the line tilts or shifts.

that equation is the model. it is a structural assumption about how the space behaves: "the vertical coordinate scales linearly with the horizontal." it is rigidly constrained, and for most actual shapes it is wrong. but it is a starting point.

---

### defining wrongness

if you pick random parameters, the line will fail to capture the target. to improve it, you first have to make the concept of "failure" mathematically precise.

for every data point `x`, the model outputs a prediction `ŷ`. you know the actual coordinate `y`. the difference `y − ŷ` is the error. 

the standard approach is to square that error and average it over the dataset. this gives a single scalar:

<div className="math-highlight">MSE = average((y − ŷ)²)</div>

this is the Mean Squared Error (MSE). squaring makes all errors positive (overshooting and undershooting do not cancel out) and penalizes large errors harder than small ones.

but why square it? why not take the absolute value? why not the fourth power? 

algorithms are rarely arbitrary. when a function like MSE becomes the universal standard, it's usually because there is a rigorous statistical assumption hiding underneath it.

suppose you assume that your data is perfectly linear, but your measurements are corrupted by random noise. specifically, you assume the noise follows a normal, Gaussian bell curve. most errors are small, and extreme errors are exponentially rare. 

under this assumption, you can ask a different question: what line makes the data you *actually observed* the most mathematically probable? this is called **Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)**. 

if you write down the probability density function for a Gaussian and run the math to maximize it for your data, all the constants factor out. because the Gaussian equation has an `e^(−x²)` term, maximizing the probability literally requires minimizing the exponent. the math collapses cleanly into exactly one final instruction: minimize the sum of the squared errors.

you aren't just "squaring it because it's positive." when you minimize the Mean Squared Error, you are implicitly claiming that your data is generated by a line corrupted by Gaussian noise, and you are analytically finding the single most probable line under that exact assumption.

---

### the loss landscape

this requires a conceptual shift.

initially, you visualize the data dots and the line in a 2D plot. drop that space entirely.

construct a new space. the axes are now the parameters, `w` and `b`. every single coordinate in this new plane represents a completely distinct line in the original space. 

for any point `(w, b)` in this parameter space, you can compute the loss `L` across the dataset. plot that loss as height along the z-axis. the result is a continuous surface. this is the **loss landscape**.

the mechanics of learning happen entirely on this surface. finding the best line for the original data is exclusively equivalent to finding the lowest point on this structural bowl.
