one of the most surprising ideas in mathematics is that any periodic function, no matter how jagged or wild, can be built by simply adding up sine and cosine waves. this is the core of the fourier series. 

think of it like mixing audio frequencies. a pure tone is a single sine wave, but a complex sound like a human voice is a sum of many frequencies at different volumes. what's remarkable is that this principle works for any repeating pattern.

---

### building a square wave

a square wave alternates instantly between `+1` and `−1`. it has sharp corners that seem impossible to capture with smooth curves. yet the formula says:

<div className="math-highlight">f(t) = (4/π) [ sin(t) + sin(3t)/3 + sin(5t)/5 + ... ]</div>

each term adds an odd harmonic, a sine wave whose frequency is an odd multiple of the fundamental. as you add more terms, the sum pulls tighter and tighter into a perfect square wave.

with 1 term, you get a pure sine wave. by 5 terms, the square shape is recognizable. crank it to 30 and you'll notice the gibbs phenomenon: the tiny overshoots near the corners never fully disappear, no matter how many terms you add. it is an unavoidable consequence of building jump discontinuities out of continuous waves.

---

### why it matters

this isn't just a mathematical trick. frequency decomposition is the foundation of digital signal processing. audio compression like MP3 throws away inaudible frequencies. JPEG compression decomposes image blocks into 2D spatial frequencies.

in the context of machine learning, fourier features are increasingly used to help neural networks learn high-frequency details that standard architectures struggle to capture.
