moving downhill in a loss landscape finds the best parameters for a model. but if the model is rigidly linear, and the data forms a complex structure like a circle, finding the "best" line does not solve the problem. the assumption itself is inadequate.

to capture complex geometry, the model must be more complex. the standard approach is to chain simple transformations together.

---

### the failure of linear stacking

a matrix multiplication is a linear map. it takes a vector and stretches, rotates, or shears it. 

to build a stronger model, the intuitive step is feeding the output of one matrix into another:

<div className="math-highlight">y = A(B(Cx))</div>

this resembles a deep network, executing three layers of computation. it appears capable of representing something much more complex than a single matrix. 

it cannot. matrix multiplication is associative. A(BC)x is strictly equivalent to (ABC)x. the product of three matrices is identically another single matrix. the entire three-layer chain collapses immediately into a single transformation y = Mx.

stacking linear maps only produces another linear map. infinite layers of matrices still represent nothing more than a flat hyper-plane. the model gains no expressivity. it remains entirely linear.

---

### breaking the collapse

to prevent the chain from collapsing, a structural barrier must be injected between the matrices to force the operations to remain distinct. 

that barrier is a non-linear function, standardly known as an **activation function**.

instead of directly feeding the output of matrix W₁ into W₂, the intermediate output passes through a non-linear operation, such as tanh:

<div className="math-highlight">y = W₂ · tanh(W₁x + b₁) + b₂</div>

because tanh warps the space non-linearly, the equation can no longer be factored down. the matrices cannot collapse. the multi-layer structure is preserved.

---

### the geometric role of non-linearities

if a matrix transformation scales and rotates space, a non-linearity is what allows the space to bend.

tanh(z) forces extreme positive values toward 1, and extreme negative values toward −1. geometrically, this operation takes an infinite unbounded space and compresses it into a finite box. points that were distant are compressed near the boundaries, introducing severe curvature.

by chaining alternating matrix multiplications and non-linearities, the network rotates the input space, compresses it, stretches it, and curves it repeatedly. this continuous warping allows structures that are impossible to separate linearly (like points inside a sphere from points outside) to be functionally unfolded until a flat plane can slice them cleanly at the final layer.

this isn't just an intuition. it is a mathematical guarantee. if you stack enough matrices and enough non-linearities, a theorem called the **Universal Approximation Theorem** proves that the network can fold the space to approximate *any* continuous function to arbitrary precision. 

a neural network is not fundamentally "learning to think." it is a universal function approximator. the architecture guarantees that the required geometry *exists* within the parameter space. the hard part, which we tackle next, is figuring out how to actually find it.
