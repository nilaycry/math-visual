the [backpropagation](/lessons/backpropagation) lesson ended with one saturated
neuron. its gradient went nearly dark while the weight downstream stayed lit.
that's the local version of the problem. what happens when you stack six of them?

each layer multiplies the gradient by the local derivative of its activation.
tanh near its limits has a derivative close to zero. multiply six of those
together and the gradient reaching the first layer isn't small. it's essentially
nothing. the first layer trains as though the loss function doesn't know it exists.

this is the vanishing gradient problem. it stopped deep networks from working
reliably for about two decades.

---

### the compounding picture

set up a concrete case. a `tanh` neuron with pre-activation `z = 2` has derivative
`σ'(2) = 1 − tanh(2)² ≈ 0.07`. chain three of them and the gradient at the first
layer is `0.07³ ≈ 0.0003`. chain six and it's about `10⁻⁷`.

<div className="math-highlight">gradient at layer k = σ'(z)^k</div>

the gradient decays exponentially with depth. it doesn't matter how good the
loss function is at the output. the signal about how to improve the first
layer can't get back there when every activation is squashing it.

### both directions fail

vanishing gradients are the tanh case: local derivatives consistently below 1, so the signal shrinks exponentially with depth. but the symmetric failure also exists.

if local derivatives are consistently above 1, the gradient grows exponentially with depth instead of shrinking. by the time it reaches the early layers, the updates are enormous. parameters overshoot. the loss explodes to NaN.

<div className="math-highlight">gradient at layer k = σ'(z)^k &nbsp; → &nbsp; grows if σ'(z) > 1, shrinks if σ'(z) < 1</div>

vanishing and exploding gradients are not separate bugs. they're the same bug in two directions: the product of many numbers less than 1 goes to zero, and the product of many numbers greater than 1 goes to infinity. the goal of every architectural and initialization trick in deep learning is to keep that product close to 1 across the depth of the network.

batch normalization keeps the pre-activations from drifting into saturated regions by normalizing each layer's output. residual connections add a direct path for the gradient to flow backward without passing through any activations. careful initialization starts weights at a scale that keeps `σ'(z)` near 1 in the first forward pass. I want to be honest about how non-obvious all of those were to find. they came from years of empirical research on exactly this failure mode, not from any theoretical necessity that's obvious in advance.

---

### the relu fix

the problem with tanh is that its derivative shrinks toward zero as the neuron
saturates. `relu` doesn't have that property. `f(z) = max(0, z)` has derivative
exactly 1 for any positive input. a chain of relu neurons with positive
activations passes the gradient through unchanged. depth doesn't compound
anything.

<div className="math-highlight">relu'(z) = 1 &nbsp; if z > 0, &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; 0 &nbsp; if z ≤ 0</div>

relu has its own failure mode. a neuron that receives a negative input on every
training example gets zero gradient forever. its weights never update. it's
called a dead neuron. how many die depends on initialization and whether early
updates push activations into negative territory.

but dead relu neurons are a recoverable problem. a network with some dead relu
neurons still trains. a network with six saturated tanh layers barely trains at
all. that asymmetry is why the switch happened, and why relu replaced tanh as
the default activation almost immediately after it was seriously tried.

---

### why it matters

knowing which failure mode you're in changes how you debug.

loss not moving for the first many steps: check whether gradients are reaching
your early layers. print gradient norms per layer, or watch the weight updates.
if the first few layers are receiving nearly zero gradient, the architecture
or initialization is the problem, not the learning rate.

loss spikes to NaN: gradient norms are probably exploding. clip them (set a
maximum norm) or switch to a more careful initialization. the loss going to NaN
almost always means multiplication is accumulating rather than shrinking.

loss descends but plateaus too early: the network may be training fine with
healthy gradients and still not have enough capacity. that's a different
problem and the tools are different.

the reason these feel similar from the outside, loss not moving, is exactly
why understanding the mechanism matters.

---

### what this connects to

fixing the local derivative problem with ReLU was only part of the solution. deep networks still struggled to train due to exploding gradients and chaotic optimization.

the architectures lesson picks up exactly here, explaining how batch normalization, residual connections, and structured initialization finally made hundred-layer networks trainable.
