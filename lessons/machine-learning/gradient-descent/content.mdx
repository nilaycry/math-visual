### the math, motivated by the geometry

the gradient is the vector of partial derivatives:

<div className="math-highlight">∇f(x₀) = [ ∂f/∂x, ∂f/∂y ] &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; (evaluated at x₀)</div>

it points in the direction of steepest ascent. but why does subtracting it guarantee we move downhill? 

stop and think about what a derivative actually is. it is a local linear approximation. if you are at a point `x₀`, the value of the function at a nearby point `x` can be approximated by a first-order Taylor expansion:

<div className="math-highlight">f(x) ≈ f(x₀) + ∇f(x₀) · (x − x₀)</div>

we want to pick a new point `x` that makes `f(x)` strictly less than `f(x₀)`. look at the equation. to guarantee the result is smaller, we must make the term `∇f(x₀) · (x − x₀)` strictly negative.

for a fixed step length, how do you make a dot product as negative as possible? you force the two vectors to point in exactly opposite directions. so the step vector `(x − x₀)` should point in the negative gradient direction.

<div className="math-highlight">x − x₀ = −α ∇f(x₀)</div>
<div className="math-highlight">x = x₀ − α ∇f(x₀)</div>

this is the update rule for gradient descent. it is not just a heuristic; it is the direction picked out by locally approximating the space as flat and asking which fixed-size step makes that approximation decrease fastest.

this also completely justifies the learning rate, `α`. the Taylor expansion is only an approximation. it only holds very close to `x₀`. if you take a massive step (a huge `α`), you walk right off the edge of the approximation, and the math no longer guarantees you went down. the learning rate bounds your step strictly to the radius where the linear geometry is actually trustworthy.

### the real problem: non-convex landscapes

the convex case is clean. real problems aren't. neural network loss
landscapes have many local minima at different depths, separated by ridges. basic
gradient descent has no memory and no foresight. it follows the slope and stops
at the first flat region it finds.

click anywhere on the surface below. the red dots are local minima.

</div>

depending on where you start, basic gradient descent finds a completely different
answer and usually not the deepest one. the gradient at a local minimum is zero, so
the update rule says it's done.

switch to **momentum** and drop the ball in the same spot. momentum uses an
exponential moving average of past gradients:

<div className="math-highlight">v_t = β · v_(t-1) + (1 − β) · ∇f(x_t)</div>
<div className="math-highlight">x_(t+1) = x_t − α · v_t</div>

`β = 0.95` means the new direction mostly remembers where you were going, with only
a small contribution from the current gradient. the ball has inertia and can punch through shallow minima that
would trap basic gradient descent.

try the **noisy** mode. the random kick on each step is what stochastic gradient
descent does in practice: computing gradients on random data subsets adds noise
that helps escape shallow traps.

neither of these is a clean fix. you can still get stuck, and the right `β` or noise scale depends on the problem.
but that's often how it goes. you use the thing that fails less often and debug when it doesn't.

---

### why it matters

large neural networks are trained by gradient-based methods. the production algorithms
usually have extra machinery, like minibatches, momentum, Adam, or AdamW, but the core
move is still the one from this lesson: compute a gradient, use it to decide how the
parameters should move, repeat. the difference between the toy version here and the
real thing is that the gradient comes from backpropagation across millions or billions
of parameters instead of from a two-variable formula. the geometry is the same.

the loss landscape of a real network is neither convex nor cleanly non-convex.
it has enormous flat regions, steep ridges, and saddle points. what makes
modern training work is less about finding the global minimum and more about
finding a basin that's wide enough to generalize to data the network hasn't
seen. gradient descent isn't solving the optimization problem cleanly. it's
navigating it well enough.

### what this doesn't cover yet

second-order methods (Newton's method, L-BFGS) that use curvature information to
take smarter steps. convergence rate analysis. the connection between gradient
flow and differential equations. in the limit of infinitesimal steps, gradient
descent becomes a continuous dynamical system, which is a completely different way
to think about optimization.
