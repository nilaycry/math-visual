probability can live comfortably without data.

statistics starts the moment you stop asking about one abstract random variable and start asking what happens when you observe one sample from a larger population.

---

### the cast of characters

the **population** is the larger process or collection you care about.

the **sample** is the finite subset you actually get to see.

a **statistic** is any function of the sample. the sample mean is a statistic. the sample variance is a statistic. so is the maximum, the median, or the number of values above a threshold.

the shift into statistics happens because the statistic is itself random before the sample is drawn.

---

### one sample is not the whole story

if you draw a sample of size `n`, compute its mean, and stop there, you have one number.

but the deeper object is the process that could have produced many such samples. every fresh sample would lead to a slightly different statistic.

the picture worth keeping is this: the population has a shape, individual samples wobble around inside it, and the statistics built from those samples have a distribution of their own.

that last object is the real plot of introductory statistics.

---

### why sampling variability matters

if two students each collect data from the same underlying population, they should not expect identical sample means. variation across samples is not a mistake in the theory. it is the thing the theory is built to quantify.

without sampling variability, there would be no need for confidence intervals, hypothesis tests, or asymptotics. one sample would already reveal the truth exactly.

---

### the first mental split to protect

there are now two distributions on stage:

- the distribution of the raw population values
- the distribution of a statistic across repeated samples

those are not the same object, and many later confusions come from swapping them without noticing.

---

### what this does not cover yet

this lesson is not yet proving how the sample mean behaves or how its variability scales with `n`.

the important thing for now is simpler: inference is not built on one dataset in isolation. it is built on the imagined repetition of the sampling process that produced it.
