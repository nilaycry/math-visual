after you choose a random variable, the next question is not "what formula do i use?"

it is: what values does this variable tend to take, and how much probability sits on each?

---

### three views of the same object

for a discrete random variable, a **probability mass function** lists point probabilities:

<div className="math-highlight">p(x) = P(X = x)</div>

for a continuous random variable, a **probability density function** is different. the density is not itself a probability at a single point. probabilities come from area:

<div className="math-highlight">P(a ≤ X ≤ b) = ∫[a,b] f(x) dx</div>

the **cumulative distribution function** works for both cases:

<div className="math-highlight">F(x) = P(X ≤ x)</div>

these are not competing definitions. they are different ways of looking at the same uncertainty.

---

### what each picture is good for

the pmf is local. it answers "what happens exactly at this value?"

the pdf is geometric. it answers "where is the density concentrated?"

the cdf is accumulative. it answers "how much probability has piled up by the time i get here?"

that is why the cdf often feels calmer than the density. it can only move upward. it does not oscillate between local spikes and empty regions. it records accumulation directly.

---

### the useful distinction

students often say "the probability is high here" when they really mean one of two different things.

- in a discrete setting, they may mean the point mass is high there
- in a continuous setting, they may mean the density is high there

those are not interchangeable statements. continuous variables do not assign positive probability to single points in the same way discrete variables do.

if you keep that distinction clear early, a lot of later confusion around densities disappears.

---

### what this does not cover yet

this lesson is deliberately skipping named families like binomial or normal. those matter, but they are examples of the bigger idea, not the idea itself.

for now, the goal is to see a distribution as a shape of uncertainty, and the pmf, pdf, and cdf as different windows onto that shape.
