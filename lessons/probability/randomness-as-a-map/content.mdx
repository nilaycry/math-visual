when a probability course says "let `X` be the number of heads," it can sound like the experiment itself has already become a number.

it hasn't. the number comes later.

first you have an experiment. then that experiment gives you a set of possible outcomes. only after that do you decide what you want to measure.

---

### the setup

imagine a simple experiment: flip two coins.

the experiment does not yet give you "the number of heads." it gives you one result from a small list of possible results.

in this case, the set of possible outcomes is:

<div className="math-highlight">&#123;HH, HT, TH, TT&#125;</div>

that is the first layer. the experiment comes with a set of possible outcomes, and when you actually perform it, one of those outcomes happens.

that is different from a measurement. `HH` is an outcome. `2` is a measurement you might later attach to it.

probability already has things to say at this level. you can ask whether you got at least one head. you can ask whether the flips matched. those are yes-or-no questions about the outcome, and each one picks out part of the outcome space.

the next layer is where the numbers appear.

and this is exactly why the extra layer is needed. the same experiment can be measured in several different ways.

you could count heads. you could record whether there was at least one head. you could record whether the two flips matched. same underlying experiment. different quantity each time.

probability needs a way to say which quantity you decided to look at. that is what the random variable is for.

---

### the map

let `X` be the number of heads.

then:

<div className="math-highlight">X(HH) = 2, X(HT) = 1, X(TH) = 1, X(TT) = 0</div>

this is what a random variable is. it is not "the randomness itself." it is the measurement rule you chose.

it takes each possible outcome and assigns it a value.

the distinction is small, but it matters. first there is the world of outcomes. then there is the measured quantity living on top of it.

when a course moves too fast, those layers get collapsed together. then the formulas start to feel like they are manipulating numbers in the dark. they aren't. the numbers came from a map.

---

### why this matters

once that split is clear, later ideas stop sounding arbitrary.

a distribution is not attached directly to "two coin flips" in some vague global sense. it is attached to the values produced by the measurement you chose.

if `X` counts heads, you get one distribution. if `Y` records whether the flips matched, you get a different one.

same experiment underneath. different map, different probabilistic object.

that is why this definition exists. not to make the notation fancy. to separate the raw source of randomness from the particular feature of it that you want to study.

expectation and variance come later. when they do, they will belong to the random variable, not to the experiment in the abstract.

---

### what this does not cover yet

this lesson is avoiding formal probability axioms on purpose. it is also postponing density functions and continuous random variables.

for now, the important move is simpler: the experiment is not the number. the number is something you build on top of the experiment so the uncertainty becomes measurable.
