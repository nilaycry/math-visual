inference is where probability language starts getting misread in very specific ways.

the fix is not more slogans. it is remembering that a confidence interval or hypothesis test is a rule applied repeatedly to fresh samples.

---

### confidence intervals are about the method

a `95%` confidence interval does **not** mean "there is a 95% chance the true parameter is in this specific realized interval."

once the sample is drawn, the interval is fixed. the unknown parameter is not wobbling around inside it.

the right statement is procedural:

<div className="math-highlight">if we repeat the sampling-and-interval-building process many times, about 95% of those intervals will cover the true parameter</div>

</div>

that is why the level belongs to the method, not to your emotional certainty about one dataset.

---

### hypothesis tests are decision rules too

a test sets up a null model, chooses a statistic, and asks whether the observed value would be unusual if the null were true.

the resulting p-value is not the probability that the null hypothesis is true. it is the probability, under the null model, of seeing data at least as extreme as what was observed.

that sounds like a technical distinction. it is actually the whole meaning.

---

### why the repeated-sampling picture matters

both intervals and tests are built to control long-run error behavior.

- confidence procedures aim for a target coverage rate
- tests aim for controlled false-positive rates under the null

if you forget the repetition story, those procedures start sounding like they are making direct epistemic claims they were never designed to make.

---

### what this does not cover yet

this lesson is stopping short of derivations, power, and alternative testing frameworks.

the main thing to keep is the interpretation discipline: confidence intervals and tests are not mystical truth-detectors. they are procedures with long-run operating behavior.
