the first time most people see the dot product, it comes with a rule: to take a · b,
you write them as vectors, flip one sideways, multiply component by component, then add.
the transposing feels arbitrary. it isn't.

---

### what the dot product is actually measuring

you probably know the projection picture: a · b measures how much a points in the
direction of b, scaled by |b|. perpendicular gives zero, parallel gives the maximum.

what's less obvious is that the component-wise formula produces exactly that geometric
quantity. the two descriptions look completely different. one is about shadows and angles.
the other is bookkeeping. they agree, and the reason they agree runs through the transpose.

---

### now the transpose

back to why you flip one sideways. if a and b are both column vectors in Rⁿ,
then aᵀb is a 1×n matrix times an n×1 matrix. the result is a 1×1 matrix,
which is just a number. that's the dot product.

<div className="math-highlight">aᵀb &nbsp;=&nbsp; a₁b₁ + a₂b₂ + ··· + aₙbₙ</div>

writing ab without the transpose would be n×1 times n×1, which isn't defined.
you could in principle invent some operation on two column vectors and call it
multiplication, but it wouldn't be matrix multiplication anymore. it would be a
new thing with its own rules, disconnected from everything else. the transpose
keeps you inside the existing framework: aᵀb is just matrix multiplication,
no special cases, all the same rules apply. that consistency is the reason.

pause here. what this is really saying is that aᵀ is a different kind of object
than a. a is a vector in Rⁿ. aᵀ is a function that eats a column vector and spits
out a number, specifically how much that vector projects onto a. every dot product is
secretly a row vector acting on a column vector. the transpose is what makes that visible.

this means the dot product is itself a matrix-vector multiplication.
aᵀ is a 1×n matrix. b is an n×1 vector. aᵀb applies the transformation aᵀ to b.
the dot product is not some separate primitive operation sitting beside linear algebra.
it is linear algebra, just with a one-dimensional output.

and bᵀa gives the same scalar. bᵀ measures projection onto b rather than onto a,
so it's a genuinely different function. but when you feed each one the other's vector
you get the same number. that's the symmetry of the dot product, now visible as a
fact about two different transformations agreeing on a specific pair of inputs.

---

### the other way to multiply two vectors

aᵀb puts the row first: 1×n times n×1, result is a scalar. now flip the order.
abᵀ puts the column first: n×1 times 1×n, result is an n×n matrix.

sit with that for a second. same two vectors, one operation swapped, and you go
from a single number to a full n×n matrix. the order of multiplication isn't
a bookkeeping detail. it completely changes what kind of object you get.

<div className="math-highlight">(abᵀ)ᵢⱼ &nbsp;=&nbsp; aᵢ · bⱼ</div>

every entry (i,j) is aᵢbⱼ. every row is a scaled copy of bᵀ. every column is a
scaled copy of a. the whole matrix points in one direction: a. it can only stretch
or shrink things along that line. this is what a rank-1 matrix looks like, the
simplest possible matrix, one direction of action.

this operation has a name, the outer product, and it almost never gets introduced
explicitly in a first course even though it's the natural counterpart to the dot product.

---

### two ways to read matrix multiplication

the dot product view and the outer product view aren't just curiosities about vectors.
they're two different ways to read any matrix multiplication, and switching between
them is genuinely useful.

the dot product view: the (i,j) entry of AB is the dot product of row i of A with
column j of B. every output entry is a projection. this is the standard definition
most courses give, and it tells you how to compute any entry in isolation.

<div className="math-highlight">(AB)ᵢⱼ = (row i of A) · (col j of B)</div>

the outer product view: AB is also equal to a sum of rank-1 matrices. take column k
of A and row k of B, form their outer product, and sum over all k. this isn't obvious
and is worth verifying yourself for a small example. each term is the simplest possible
matrix, one direction of action. SVD does exactly this decomposition, with the
directions chosen to be as efficient as possible, largest contribution first.

---

### two ways to read Ax

the same split applies to matrix-vector multiplication. given Ax, there are two
equally valid ways to see what's happening.

the row view: the i-th output entry is row i of A dotted with x. each component
of Ax is a projection. the matrix is measuring x against each of its rows and
reporting the results.

the column view: Ax is a linear combination of the columns of A, with the entries
of x as weights.

<div className="math-highlight">Ax = x₁(col₁) + x₂(col₂) + ··· + xₙ(colₙ)</div>

both are exactly right. the row view is natural for thinking about projections and
measurements. the column view is natural for thinking about what vectors Ax can
possibly be, which is the column space.

i found the column view genuinely surprising the first time I saw it stated carefully,
even though I had been computing Ax for months. it reframes the question from
"how do I compute this output" to "what combination of directions am I building."

---

### things worth knowing that nobody mentions

every linear function from Rⁿ to R can be written as a dot product with something.
that something is always a row vector. so row vectors aren't just column vectors
written sideways. they're a different kind of object: functions that eat vectors
and return numbers. the row vectors note goes into this properly.

there is also a deeper way to define the transpose that has nothing to do with
flipping indices. for any matrix A, the transpose is the unique matrix satisfying
(Ax)·y = x·(Aᵀy) for all vectors x and y. in words: applying A then dotting with y
gives the same result as dotting with Aᵀy directly. this identity is worth verifying
on a small example. it's how the transpose is actually defined in more advanced settings,
and it's a more honest description of what transposing is than "swap rows and columns."
