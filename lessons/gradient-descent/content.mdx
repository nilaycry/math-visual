### the math, motivated by the geometry

The gradient is the vector of partial derivatives:

<div className="math-highlight">∇f(x, y) = [ ∂f/∂x, ∂f/∂y ]</div>

It points in the direction of steepest ascent. To convince yourself of this formula consider first thinking about the
vector that points in the direction of steepest ascent. now think about how would you find the components of this vector. 

<div className="math-highlight">x_n+1 = x_n − α · ∇f(x_n)</div>

"small enough" really depends on the curvature. The maximum eigenvalue of
the Hessian (the matrix of second derivatives) sets the upper bound on α before
you overshoot (really cool math worth reading). High curvature means smaller steps. This is why the same learning
rate that works on one problem breaks on another.

### the real problem: non-convex landscapes

the convex case is clean. real problems aren't. Neural network loss
landscapes have many local minima at different depths, separated by ridges. basic
gradient descent has no memory and no foresight. it follows the slope and stops
at the first flat region it finds.

click anywhere on the surface below. the red dots are local minima. the green
cross is the global minimum.

<div className="not-prose my-8">
  <NonConvexSketch />
</div>

depending on where you start, basic gradient descent finds a completely different
answer and usually not the deepest one. The gradient at a local minimum is zero, so
the update rule says it's done. i find the ways we overcome this limitation pretty cool

switch to **momentum** and drop the ball in the same spot. Momentum adds an
exponential moving average of past gradients to the update:

<div className="math-highlight">v_t = β · v_{t-1} + ∇f(x_t)</div>
<div className="math-highlight">x_{t+1} = x_t − α · v_t</div>

β = 0.9 means 90% of the step comes from where you were going, 10% from the
current gradient. The ball has inertia — it can punch through shallow minima that
would trap basic gradient descent.

try the **noisy** mode. The random kick on each step is what stochastic gradient
descent does in practice: computing gradients on random data subsets adds noise
that helps escape shallow traps. its not a perfect solution but does better than the baseline.

### what this doesn't cover yet

Second-order methods (Newton's method, L-BFGS) that use curvature information to
take smarter steps. Convergence rate analysis. The connection between gradient
flow and differential equations. in the limit of infinitesimal steps, gradient
descent becomes a continuous dynamical system, which is a completely different way
to think about optimization.
