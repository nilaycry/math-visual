the first strange thing about a class like this is that the syllabus looks scattered.
logic. set theory. functions. counting. modular arithmetic. relations. induction.
it can feel like someone dumped a bag of unrelated prerequisites onto the table and
called that a course.

I don't think that's what is happening.

the real course is the shift from "can I do this calculation" to "can I justify this
claim." every chapter is really a different place where that same skill gets used.
logic teaches you what a statement is saying. set theory gives you a language precise
enough to state definitions cleanly. functions and relations teach you to pay attention
to what maps are allowed to do. induction teaches you how to prove infinitely many
statements without checking them one at a time.

if you read the course that way, it stops looking scattered.

---

### what usually feels different

in calculus or algebra, a wrong answer often looks wrong numerically. you differentiate
incorrectly and the graph stops matching. you row reduce badly and the system no longer
solves.

proofs are slipperier. a sentence can sound convincing while hiding a gap, a circular
step, or a quantifier shift that changes the entire meaning. that's the part that took
me a while to respect. in a proof-based class, "that seems true" and "that has been
shown" are nowhere near the same thing.

so the first job is not becoming more clever. it's becoming more careful.

---

### what to look for while reading

when you read a theorem, ask three questions before you touch the proof.

what are the hypotheses. what exactly is being assumed. what is the conclusion. what
exactly must be shown. and what would count as evidence here. an example, a direct
argument, a contradiction, a construction.

that sounds small, but it changes everything. a lot of confusion in this course comes
from trying to prove a stronger claim than the theorem asks for, or from using an
assumption that was never actually given.

pause here. if a proof ever feels like it came out of nowhere, go back and separate the
hypotheses from the conclusion by hand. half the time the proof strategy is already
hidden in that split.

### what to expect from these notes

I'm going to keep the notes short and sequential. one idea at a time, roughly in the
order the course uses it. when a definition is doing subtle work, I want to say that
out loud instead of pretending it is obvious.

this is not meant to replace the class text. it is more like a running companion for the
moment when the formal version is technically clear but not yet mentally stable.

---

### the skill underneath all of it

the unifying skill in math 314 is controlled translation.

you translate a sentence in English into logical form. you translate a membership claim
into set notation. you translate "injective" into the exact pattern of equalities you are
allowed to use. you translate an infinite family of statements into the base case plus the
inductive step.

the stronger you get at those translations, the less mysterious proofs feel.

that is also why this course matters so much. abstract algebra, real analysis, topology,
all of them assume you can already do this.
