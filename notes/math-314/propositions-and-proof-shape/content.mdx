before you can write a proof, you need a sentence that can actually be true or false.
that sounds too basic to matter, until you notice how many mathematical sentences are
not complete enough to carry a truth value yet.

`x = 2` is not a proposition by itself. neither is "take an integer $n$." neither is
"is this always true?" they might become part of a proposition later, but on their own
they are missing context.

that is the first filter in this course. before asking whether something is provable,
ask whether it is even a proposition.

---

### propositions versus open sentences

a **proposition** is a statement with a definite truth value. it is either true or false.

$3 \mid 12$ is a proposition. so is "$5$ is even." the second one happens to be false,
but it is still a proposition because it has a definite truth value.

an **open sentence** has variables whose roles have not been fixed yet. the sentence
$x^2 = 4$ does not have a truth value until you say what $x$ is allowed to be. if
$x = 2$, it is true. if $x = 3$, it is false. until the variable is pinned down or
quantified, there is nothing to prove.

I think this is one of the earliest places where proof courses feel annoying on purpose.
you want the math to start, and instead someone is asking whether your sentence is even
grammatical. but that fussiness is doing real work.

---

### logical connectives are not decoration

once you have propositions, you can combine them. "and," "or," "not," "if ... then ..."
these are not stylistic choices. they change the conditions under which a statement is
true, so they change what a proof has to do.

if a theorem says "$P$ and $Q$," then both pieces matter. if it says "$P$ or $Q$," you
need to know whether the proof uses one branch, both branches, or a case split. if it says
"if $P$, then $Q$," then a proof usually starts by assuming $P$ and works toward $Q$.

this is the part that seems trivial until a proof fails because "or" was treated like
"and," or because the conclusion was used as if it were an assumption.

---

### what a proof of an implication is really doing

most first theorems in this course look like implications:

$$P \implies Q$$

to prove that, you are not showing that $P$ is true in the universe. you are showing
that **if** $P$ is true, then $Q$ must follow.

that distinction matters a lot. suppose the theorem says:

> if $n$ is even, then $n^2$ is even.

the proof begins by assuming $n$ is even. not because we know every integer is even,
but because that is the hypothesis we have been offered. from there you use the definition
of even:

$$n = 2k \text{ for some integer } k$$

then

$$n^2 = (2k)^2 = 4k^2 = 2(2k^2)$$

and since $2k^2$ is an integer, $n^2$ is even.

notice the shape. assume the hypothesis. unpack the definition. do algebra that keeps
track of what must remain an integer. arrive at the conclusion.

that shape shows up constantly.

---

### the hidden role of definitions

proofs in early chapters often feel underwhelming once written out. "that was it?"

usually the answer is yes. the hard part was not some clever manipulation. the hard part
was knowing which definition to expand and what form of the definition would actually move
the argument.

even means $2k$. odd means $2k + 1$. injective means equal outputs force equal inputs.
subset means every element on the left is also on the right.

pause here. when a proof feels impossible, there is a decent chance the next move is not
to be more inventive. it is to write down the exact definition of the word that is carrying
the statement.

---

### a proof is a controlled path

I like thinking of a proof as a path with guardrails. the theorem tells you where you may
start and where you must end. the definitions tell you what moves are legal in between.

good proofs rarely feel like random inspiration after the fact. they feel constrained.
once you know the hypotheses, the conclusion, and the relevant definitions, the search
space gets much smaller.

that is also why reading proofs carefully matters. a proof is not just a certificate that
the theorem is true. it is an example of how those constraints were navigated.
