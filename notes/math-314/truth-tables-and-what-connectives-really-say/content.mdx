once you stop treating mathematical statements as just English sentences, the connectives
start to matter a lot more.

"and." "or." "not." "if ... then ..."

those are not decoration. they control when a statement counts as true, which means they
control what a proof is allowed to assume and what it still has to show. a lot of early
proof mistakes are really connective mistakes in disguise.

---

### why truth tables matter at all

a truth table looks mechanical at first. you write down every possible truth value for
the component statements, then compute the truth value of the compound statement.

that can feel beneath the level of actual mathematics. I used to think that too. but the
point is not the table itself. the point is that the table forces you to stop relying on
vibes about what a statement "sounds like" and instead say exactly when it is true.

for a proof-based course, that precision is the whole game.

---

### conjunction and disjunction

if $P$ and $Q$ are propositions, then:

- $P \land Q$ means "$P$ and $Q$"
- $P \lor Q$ means "$P$ or $Q$"

the connective $\land$ is strict. the statement $P \land Q$ is true only when both parts
are true. if even one part fails, the whole conjunction fails.

the connective $\lor$ is looser. in this course, $P \lor Q$ is true when at least one of
the two statements is true. that includes the case where both are true.

this is the first place where ordinary English causes trouble. in everyday speech, "or"
sometimes suggests a choice between two incompatible options. in logic, it does not. it is
an inclusive or.

pause here. if a theorem assumes "$P$ or $Q$," that does not mean you are allowed to pick
whichever one feels easier and forget the other exists. it means the proof may need cases.

---

### negation

the connective $\lnot P$ means "not $P$." its truth value is the easiest one:

- if $P$ is true, then $\lnot P$ is false
- if $P$ is false, then $\lnot P$ is true

that sounds too simple to mention, but negation gets harder the moment the statement being
negated has internal structure. "not $P$" is easy. "not ($P \land Q$)" is already a
different kind of object. that is where the next note is going.

for now, the important point is that negation flips truth value. it does not automatically
tell you what the new sentence should look like in words. you have to rewrite it carefully.

---

### the conditional is the one people mistrust

the statement

$$P \implies Q$$

means "if $P$, then $Q$."

the first two rows of its truth table feel natural:

- if $P$ is true and $Q$ is true, then the conditional is true
- if $P$ is true and $Q$ is false, then the conditional is false

that second row is the one a proof is trying to rule out. a theorem of the form
$P \implies Q$ is really saying: there is no case where the hypothesis holds and the
conclusion fails.

the strange part is what happens when $P$ is false. in logic, the conditional counts as
true in both remaining cases. that is the part people usually dislike the first time.

I think the cleanest way to say it is this: a conditional only makes a promise about the
worlds where the hypothesis actually happens. if the hypothesis never occurs, the statement
has not been broken.

---

### why that weird definition is actually useful

suppose the statement is:

> if an integer $n$ is divisible by $4$, then $n$ is even.

if $n = 12$, the hypothesis and conclusion are both true. good.

if $n = 6$, the hypothesis is false but the conclusion is true. the conditional still
counts as true, because the statement never promised anything about numbers that are not
divisible by $4$.

if $n = 7$, the hypothesis is false and the conclusion is false. the conditional still
counts as true for the same reason.

the only way the statement fails is if you produce some integer divisible by $4$ that is
not even. that is exactly the counterexample pattern for an implication.

the truth table is not arbitrary here. it matches the proof behavior we want.

---

### what a connective tells a proof to do

once you know the connective, you already know something about proof shape.

if the claim is $P \land Q$, you must show both parts.

if the claim is $P \lor Q$, you must justify at least one branch, and sometimes you need
to decide carefully which branch you can actually prove.

if the claim is $P \implies Q$, you usually begin by assuming $P$ and work toward $Q$.

if the claim is $\lnot P$, you are often trying to show that $P$ would lead somewhere
impossible, though the exact method depends on context.

the connective is already telling you the shape of the argument. that is the part worth
remembering after the truth tables themselves fade.

---

### one subtle distinction that keeps coming back

do not confuse a statement with its converse.

$P \implies Q$ and $Q \implies P$ are different claims. one can be true while the other is
false. "if a number is divisible by $4$, then it is even" is true. "if a number is even,
then it is divisible by $4$" is false.

this seems obvious in a simple arithmetic example. it becomes much less obvious once the
statements get abstract, which is why it is worth building the reflex now.
