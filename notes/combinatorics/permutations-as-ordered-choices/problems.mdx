### recognizing the object

**1.** For each question, say whether the objects being counted are ordered lists,
unordered selections, or something else. Do not compute the answer yet.

a. How many ways are there to award gold, silver, and bronze medals to three people
from a group of $12$ finalists?

b. How many committees of $3$ people can be chosen from a group of $12$ finalists?

c. How many four-letter strings can be made from the letters $a,b,c,d,e$ if no letter
is repeated?

d. How many subsets of size $4$ does $\{a,b,c,d,e\}$ have?

<Solution>

**the move.** decide whether position matters.

a. ordered list. gold, silver, bronze are different positions.

b. unordered selection. a committee has no first, second, or third member unless the
problem adds roles.

c. ordered list. strings have positions.

d. unordered selection. a subset has no order.

</Solution>

**2.** Let $S = \{a,b,c,d\}$. List all $2$-permutations of $S$.

Then explain why $ab$ and $ba$ are different objects, but $aa$ is not an object in
this set.

### direct permutation counts

**3.** Compute each quantity, and write one sentence explaining what kind of ordered
list it counts.

a. $P(8,3)$

b. $P(10,10)$

c. $P(6,0)$

d. $P(n,1)$ for $n \in \mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}$

**4.** How many license plates can be made if a plate consists of three distinct
uppercase letters followed by two distinct digits?

Assume the letters are chosen from a $26$-letter alphabet and the digits are chosen
from $\{0,1,\ldots,9\}$.

<Solution>

Choose the letters first, then the digits. The letters form a $3$-permutation of a
$26$-element set, and the digits form a $2$-permutation of a $10$-element set.

$$P(26,3)P(10,2) = (26 \cdot 25 \cdot 24)(10 \cdot 9).$$

The multiplication principle applies because the letter choices and digit choices are
independent stages.

</Solution>

**5.** How many injective functions $f:\{1,2,3,4\} \to \{a,b,c,d,e,f,g\}$ are there?

Explain why this is a permutation problem.

<Solution>

An injective function assigns distinct outputs to the four inputs. Once the domain is
ordered as $1,2,3,4$, the function is determined by the ordered list

$$(f(1), f(2), f(3), f(4)).$$

The entries are distinct elements of a $7$-element codomain, so the number is

$$P(7,4).$$

</Solution>

### restrictions

**6.** How many four-digit numbers have distinct digits and no digit equal to $0$?

**7.** How many four-digit numbers have distinct digits?

Be careful: the first digit cannot be $0$, but the later digits can be.

<Solution>

Choose the thousands digit first. It has $9$ choices: $1,2,\ldots,9$.

After that, choose the remaining three digits from the remaining $9$ digits, since $0$
is now allowed but the first digit cannot be repeated. This gives

$$9 \cdot P(9,3).$$

Equivalently,

$$9 \cdot 9 \cdot 8 \cdot 7.$$

</Solution>

**8.** How many five-letter strings can be made from the letters $a,b,c,d,e,f,g$ if no
letter is repeated and the string must begin with a vowel?

For this problem, take the vowels to be $a$ and $e$.

**9.** How many seven-digit numbers can be made from the digits $1,2,\ldots,9$, with
no repeated digit, if $5$ and $6$ must appear consecutively in either order?

**10.** How many seven-digit numbers can be made from the digits $1,2,\ldots,9$, with
no repeated digit, if $5$ and $6$ do not appear consecutively?

<Solution>

Problem 9 is the complement count used in the note. Treat $56$ or $65$ as an ordered
block. There are $2$ internal orders for the block and $6$ possible starting positions
for a two-position block inside a seven-position number. The remaining five positions
are filled by a $5$-permutation of the seven digits

$$\{1,2,3,4,7,8,9\}.$$

So the count for Problem 9 is

$$2 \cdot 6 \cdot P(7,5).$$

For Problem 10, subtract from all seven-digit numbers with distinct digits:

$$P(9,7) - 2 \cdot 6 \cdot P(7,5).$$

</Solution>

### circular arrangements

**11.** How many ways are there to seat $8$ distinct people around a round table, if
two arrangements that differ only by rotation are considered the same?

**12.** How many ways are there to seat $8$ distinct people around a round table if
Alice and Ben must sit next to each other?

<Solution>

Treat Alice and Ben as one block. Then there are $7$ objects around the table: the
block plus the other $6$ people. Circularly arranging these $7$ objects gives

$$(7-1)! = 6!$$

arrangements. Inside the block, Alice and Ben can appear in $2$ orders. So the total is

$$2 \cdot 6!.$$

</Solution>

**13.** How many ways are there to seat $8$ distinct people around a round table if
Alice and Ben must not sit next to each other?

Write this as a subtraction problem.

### explain the count

**14.** A student writes that the number of circular arrangements of $n$ distinct
objects is $n!/n$ because "we divide by rotations."

Explain what has to be checked before that division is valid.

**15.** A student tries to count seven-digit numbers from $\{1,2,\ldots,9\}$ with no
repeated digit and with $5$ and $6$ not consecutive by first choosing the position of
$5$, then choosing the position of $6$, then filling the rest.

Why does this approach require cases?

<Solution>

The number of positions available for $6$ depends on where $5$ is placed. If $5$ is in
an endpoint position, then only one position is forbidden for $6$. If $5$ is in an
interior position, then two positions are forbidden for $6$.

So the phrase "choose the position of $6$" does not have one fixed number of choices
unless the placement of $5$ has already been split into endpoint and interior cases.

</Solution>
