combinatorics looks simple at first. how many ways can you arrange $n$ things.
how many subsets does a set of size $n$ have. easy questions with short answers.

then the problems get stranger. you're not counting arrangements anymore, you're
counting something whose count reveals a structural fact about the objects themselves.
the number of spanning trees in a complete graph is $n^{n-2}$, which seems like
it should be a coincidence but isn't. the number of ways to tile a $2 \times n$
board with dominoes follows a fibonacci recurrence, which seems like it should be
surprising but becomes obvious once you see it the right way.

that shift, from "how many" to "why that number, and what does it tell us," is
what these notes are about.

---

### what math 413 actually covers

the course runs through several big ideas: basic counting principles (multiplication,
addition, bijections), binomial coefficients and their identities, inclusion-exclusion,
generating functions, recurrences, and graph theory.

those topics look scattered on a syllabus. they're not. the thread connecting them
is the idea of a bijection: if you can find a one-to-one correspondence between two
sets, they have the same size, and you've proven a counting identity without counting.
a lot of combinatorics is secretly finding the right bijection.

generating functions are the other big unifying idea. they turn recurrences into
algebra and make impossible-looking sums routine.

### how I'm writing these notes

same structure as my abstract algebra notes. one idea per note, in the order the
course covers it. I try to lead with a picture or a concrete example before giving
the general statement.

combinatorics has a reputation for being tricks. I don't think that's quite right.
the tricks are real, but they come from a small number of deep ideas that keep
reappearing in different clothes. I try to name those ideas when I see them instead
of treating each problem as its own isolated puzzle.

these are my notes from math 413 at uiuc. they're not a textbook. they're how I
made sense of it.
