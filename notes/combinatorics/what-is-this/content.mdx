combinatorics is the part of mathematics that asks what can happen when the world is
finite.

how many routes are there through a city grid? how many ways can a group be arranged?
when is a task impossible? how much structure is forced once there are enough objects
in too few places?

at first, this sounds like arithmetic. count the things, get the answer, move on. but
the longer I sit with combinatorics, the more it feels like a subject about seeing.
you learn to look at a messy collection of possibilities and ask what kind of object
it really is.

is it a list? a subset? a path? a coloring? a partition? a sequence? a graph?

once you choose the right description, the problem often changes completely.

---

### why study it

combinatorics is worth studying because it trains a very specific kind of precision:
count each object once, and only once.

that sounds modest, but it is not. it forces you to say what counts as one object,
when two objects are different, what has been excluded, and whether your description
has secretly counted the same thing several times.

this habit shows up everywhere finite structure shows up. probability needs it,
because probabilities often begin as ratios of counts. computer science needs it,
because algorithms, graphs, strings, trees, and networks are finite objects.
algebra and geometry use it whenever a complicated object can be encoded by simpler
discrete data.

but the deeper reason is that combinatorics has a particular kind of surprise. a
tiling problem turns into the Fibonacci numbers. a question about handshakes becomes
a theorem about graphs. a binomial identity becomes obvious once both sides are seen
as counting the same set.

the subject keeps showing you that a problem may have a hidden shape.

---

### a little history

people have counted arrangements, games, and patterns for a very long time. Pascal's
triangle appeared in several mathematical traditions before it became attached to
Pascal's name. counting arguments were tied to gambling and probability in the work
of Pascal and Fermat. Euler's solution of the Konigsberg bridge problem helped open
the door to graph theory.

modern combinatorics grew from many such sources: probability, recreational puzzles,
geometry, algebra, graph theory, and later computer science. that mixed origin is
part of the flavor of the subject. it does not feel like one straight road. it feels
like a set of techniques that kept appearing wherever finite structure mattered.

Brualdi's book begins with that variety on purpose. chessboards, magic squares,
routes, circles, games. the point is not that all of these examples use the same
trick. the point is that each one asks us to replace an informal situation with a
precise finite object.

---

### what these notes are for

these notes are my attempt to learn that replacement process slowly.

I do not want the course to become a bag of formulas. formulas matter, but only after
we understand what they are counting. so the notes will usually start with the object:
what is being chosen, arranged, grouped, colored, or encoded?

then we ask why the count is exact.

that is the main promise of combinatorics. if you describe the objects clearly
enough, a problem that first looked like a pile of cases can become something almost
inevitable.
