---
title: "the least upper bound property"
description: "the completeness statement that quietly supports the real line"
week: 1
---

one of the fastest ways to tell whether a course has shifted from calculus to analysis
is that the real numbers stop being treated as obvious.

in calculus, $\mathbb{R}$ is usually the background. points move along it, functions live
on it, intervals sit inside it. in analysis, we eventually have to say what property of
$\mathbb{R}$ makes those arguments work.

one answer is completeness, and the first concrete form of completeness is the least
upper bound property.

I like starting here because it makes the course honest early. if later theorems about
limits are going to lean on something special about $\mathbb{R}$, I would rather name
that special thing now than keep treating the real line like a magical backdrop.

---

### the definition

let $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}$. an **upper bound** for $S$ is a real number $M$ such that

$$x \le M \quad \text{for every } x \in S.$$

the set $S$ is **bounded above** if it has at least one upper bound.

a **least upper bound** of $S$ is an upper bound $u$ such that every upper bound $M$ of
$S$ satisfies

$$u \le M.$$

in other words, $u$ is the smallest number that still stays above the whole set. we
usually call it the **supremum** and write $u = \sup S$.

---

### the property itself

the **least upper bound property** says:

> **Definition.** Every nonempty subset of $\mathbb{R}$ that is bounded above has a least upper bound in $\mathbb{R}$.

this statement is not true in every ordered number system. that is why it matters. it is
not just a vocabulary word. it is a structural guarantee.

when I first saw this definition, it looked too formal to be important. but analysis
keeps coming back to exactly this kind of guarantee: if a set is pushing upward toward a
boundary, $\mathbb{R}$ does not make you guess whether that boundary exists.

---

### first examples

consider the interval

$$S = (0,1).$$

the number $1$ is an upper bound. so is $2$, or $100$. but the least upper bound is $1$,
even though $1 \notin S$.

that example matters because it separates two ideas that are easy to blur:

- a maximum element of the set
- the least upper bound of the set

here the set has no maximum, but it still has a supremum.

that distinction took me a second the first time I saw it. "largest element" and "best
upper bound" sound almost identical in conversation, but analysis needs them separated
completely.

---

### why this is the right place to start

later, when a sequence converges or a monotone bounded sequence has a limit, the proof
often builds a set of partial information and then asks for the best possible boundary
point. the supremum is that boundary point.

so even before the full epsilon language arrives, this property tells us something about
how $\mathbb{R}$ behaves: bounded data does not leave us with a gap right where the next
number ought to be.

to me, that is the real point of the note. the supremum is not just a definition to
memorize. it is one of the first signs that completeness is a usable mathematical
promise.

---

### proof shape to practice

when working with suprema, a common proof pattern is to show two facts:

> **Claim.** To prove that a number $u$ is $\sup S$, it is enough to show:
>
> 1. $u$ is an upper bound for $S$.
> 2. Every number smaller than $u$ fails to be an upper bound for $S$.

the first part says $u$ is high enough. the second part says it is not unnecessarily
higher than it needs to be.

that second sentence is often where the real work is. you usually pick an arbitrary
$v < u$ and then produce some element of $S$ with

$$v < x \le u.$$

that single move shows $v$ was too small to control the set.

I find this worth pausing on because it is a proof shape that comes back a lot. analysis
often proves an extremal statement by showing that anything slightly smaller or slightly
larger breaks.
