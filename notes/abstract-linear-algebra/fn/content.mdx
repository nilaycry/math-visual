if you've taken a course that builds mathematics from the ground up, something about the
vector space axioms might feel off. in that setting, commutativity of addition isn't
assumed. it's proved. you define addition on $\mathbb{N}$ recursively using the Peano
axioms, then prove $a + b = b + a$ by induction. same with associativity. these are
theorems, not starting points.

so why does linear algebra just write down "commutativity: $u + v = v + u$" and move on?

---

### two different kinds of work

the confusion comes from mixing up two different levels of mathematics.

at the **foundational level**, you're constructing specific objects: the natural numbers,
the integers, the reals. you start from almost nothing (the Peano axioms, or set theory)
and build up. commutativity of addition on $\mathbb{N}$ is a fact you derive about a
specific, concretely defined thing. it's a theorem because you had to earn it.

at the **structural level**, you're doing something different. you're not constructing a
specific object. you're defining a class of objects by listing the properties you want
them to have. the vector space axioms don't make a claim about the world. they say: I'm
interested in studying structures that satisfy these eight rules. commutativity is a
requirement, not a theorem, because we're defining what counts as a vector space.

the axioms are a filter, not a foundation.

---

### what happens when you check an example

when you verify that $\mathbb{R}^n$ is a vector space, you're back to proving. you have
a specific construction: lists of real numbers with componentwise operations. you then
check each axiom. for commutativity:

$$(x_1, \ldots, x_n) + (y_1, \ldots, y_n) = (x_1 + y_1, \ldots, x_n + y_n) = (y_1 + x_1, \ldots, y_n + x_n)$$

that last step uses commutativity of addition in $\mathbb{R}$, which was established
at the foundational level. so the proof exists. it was done elsewhere, once, for $\mathbb{R}$, and now we're allowed to use it.

the same happens for every example. polynomials, matrices, functions. for each one, you
prove the axioms hold by reducing each check to something already known.

---

### what this buys you

once you've verified that some object is a vector space, every theorem you prove about
abstract vector spaces applies to it automatically. you don't re-prove anything.

I find this the most compelling reason for the abstraction. you prove a theorem once,
in full generality, from just the eight axioms. that theorem is then simultaneously a
fact about $\mathbb{R}^n$, about polynomials, about continuous functions, about every
other vector space you'll ever meet. the filter is what gives you the leverage.

---

### the short answer

in discrete math, commutativity was a claim about a specific object that had to be
proved. in linear algebra, it's a requirement on whatever you're calling a vector space.
when you check that $\mathbb{R}^n$ qualifies, you do prove it, using the commutativity
of $\mathbb{R}$ that was already established. the proof didn't disappear. it's upstream.
