abstract linear algebra is a proof-based course. that means the goal isn't to compute
answers but to explain why things are true. if you've taken discrete math or an intro
proofs course, you already have most of what you need. this note is a quick map of
what gets used most often.

if you haven't seen formal proofs before, this is the hardest part of the adjustment.
not the linear algebra itself, but the shift from "get the right number" to "write a
convincing argument." that shift takes time. it took me a while.

---

### how proofs actually work

a proof is an argument that leaves no gaps. you start from things you know are true
and apply rules that preserve truth until you reach what you wanted to show. every step
has to be justified, either by a definition, a previously proved theorem, or a basic
logical rule.

in practice, most proofs in this course use one of four patterns.

**direct proof.** assume the hypothesis, then derive the conclusion. the default. use
it unless you have a reason not to.

**proof by contrapositive.** to show "if $P$ then $Q$," prove instead "if not $Q$ then
not $P$." the two statements are logically equivalent, so this is not cheating. it
helps when the contrapositive is easier to work with than the original.

**proof by contradiction.** assume the thing you want to prove is false, then derive a
contradiction. this tells you the assumption was wrong, so the original statement must
be true. useful when you don't know how to construct the conclusion directly, but you
know what goes wrong if it fails.

**induction.** for statements about natural numbers. prove the base case, then prove
that if the statement holds for $n$ it holds for $n+1$. the two together cover every
natural number.

### sets and functions

these come up constantly, so the notation should feel automatic.

$A \subseteq B$ means every element of $A$ is also in $B$. to prove this, take an
arbitrary element of $A$ and show it's in $B$. to prove $A = B$, prove $A \subseteq B$
and $B \subseteq A$.

a **function** $f: A \to B$ assigns to each element of $A$ exactly one element of $B$.
three properties matter in this course:

- **injective** (one-to-one): $f(x) = f(y)$ implies $x = y$. different inputs give
  different outputs.
- **surjective** (onto): for every $b \in B$, there exists $a \in A$ with $f(a) = b$.
  every output is achieved.
- **bijective**: both. injective and surjective together. this is what you need for
  a function to have an inverse.

the standard proof that $f$ is injective: assume $f(x) = f(y)$, then derive $x = y$.
for surjective: take an arbitrary $b \in B$, construct an $a$ with $f(a) = b$.

### quantifiers

"for all" and "there exists" are everywhere in definitions and theorems. mixing them
up is the most common source of confusion.

$\forall x \in S, P(x)$ means $P(x)$ is true for every element of $S$. to prove this,
take an arbitrary $x$ and show $P(x)$ holds without assuming anything special about $x$.

$\exists x \in S, P(x)$ means at least one element of $S$ satisfies $P$. to prove this,
find one. exhibit it explicitly if you can, or argue it must exist.

the negation of $\forall x, P(x)$ is $\exists x, \neg P(x)$. the negation of
$\exists x, P(x)$ is $\forall x, \neg P(x)$. this matters when you're writing a proof
by contradiction.

### when you're stuck

when I'm stuck, usually it's because I haven't stared at the definitions long enough.
write them all out — every condition, every clause. if you're proving something about a
vector space, list all eight axioms. if it's about a linear map, write out both conditions
of linearity. the proof often falls out directly.

if it doesn't, try a small example. pick specific vectors, specific scalars. see what
the statement is actually saying in a case you can check by hand. then ask why it's
true in that case, and whether that reason generalizes.

the other thing: a proof doesn't have to be elegant on the first try. write the rough
argument, make sure it's correct, then clean it up. the version you hand in and the
version you first wrote are usually very different.
