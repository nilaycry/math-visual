the previous note ended with a process: take a spanning list, remove redundant vectors
one by one until none remain. what you're left with is a list that spans $V$ and has no
redundancy. that object deserves a name.

---

### the definition

> **Definition.** A **basis** of $V$ is a list of vectors in $V$ that is linearly
> independent and spans $V$.

that's it. two conditions, both of which we already understand. a basis is the leanest
possible description of a space: enough vectors to reach everything, no vector wasted.

what I found surprising at first is that there's no uniqueness in the definition. a
vector space can have many different bases. $\mathbb{R}^2$ has the standard basis
$(1,0), (0,1)$, but also $(1,1), (1,-1)$, and infinitely many others. the definition
doesn't single out a preferred one.

what *is* unique is the size. every basis of a given finite-dimensional space has the
same number of vectors. but that's the next note. first, let's understand why bases
are useful at all.

---

### unique representation

> **Theorem.** A list $v_1, \ldots, v_n$ is a basis of $V$ if and only if every vector
> $v \in V$ can be written as
>
> $$v = a_1 v_1 + \cdots + a_n v_n$$
>
> for a **unique** choice of scalars $a_1, \ldots, a_n \in \mathbf{F}$.

existence of such a representation is just spanning: if the list spans $V$, every vector
is some linear combination. the interesting half is uniqueness, and that's where
independence earns its place.

suppose $v = a_1 v_1 + \cdots + a_n v_n$ and also $v = b_1 v_1 + \cdots + b_n v_n$.
subtract the two expressions:

$$\mathbf{0} = (a_1 - b_1) v_1 + \cdots + (a_n - b_n) v_n$$

since the list is linearly independent, the only solution is $a_i - b_i = 0$ for all
$i$, meaning $a_i = b_i$. the two representations were the same all along.

pause here. this is what coordinates actually are. when you write a vector in
$\mathbb{R}^3$ as $(2, -1, 5)$, you're recording the unique scalars in its expansion
relative to the standard basis. the basis is what makes coordinates meaningful: without
a basis, a vector has no natural "address." with one, every vector has exactly one.

---

### examples

**the standard basis of $\mathbb{R}^n$.** the list $e_1, \ldots, e_n$ where $e_k$ has a
$1$ in position $k$ and $0$s elsewhere. it spans $\mathbb{R}^n$ since any $(x_1, \ldots,
x_n) = x_1 e_1 + \cdots + x_n e_n$, and it's independent since that representation is
clearly unique. the scalars are just the coordinates themselves.

**another basis of $\mathbb{R}^2$.** the list $(1, 1), (1, -1)$ is also a basis. to
check: $(a+b, a-b) = (x, y)$ has the unique solution $a = (x+y)/2$, $b = (x-y)/2$ for
any $(x,y)$. different basis, different coordinates, same space.

**polynomials.** in $\mathcal{P}_m(\mathbf{F})$, the list $1, x, x^2, \ldots, x^m$ is a
basis. any polynomial of degree at most $m$ is uniquely expressed as $a_0 + a_1 x + \cdots + a_m x^m$. the coefficients are exactly the coordinates relative to this basis.

---

### a non-example

$(1,0), (0,1), (1,1)$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$ spans the space but is not a basis, because it's
linearly dependent. the vector $(2, 2)$ has more than one representation:

$$2(1,0) + 2(0,1) + 0(1,1) = (2,2)$$
$$1(1,0) + 1(0,1) + 1(1,1) = (2,2)$$

adding a redundant vector breaks uniqueness. independence isn't just a nicety. without
it, the "address" a basis is supposed to provide stops being well-defined.

---

### every finite-dimensional space has a basis

a **finite-dimensional** vector space is one that is spanned by some finite list of
vectors. given any such spanning list, the linear dependence lemma from the previous note
says you can remove vectors one at a time, without shrinking the span, until the list is
independent. what remains is a basis.

so any finite-dimensional space has at least one basis. this isn't obvious: the
definition of basis is a conjunction of two conditions, and it takes an argument to show
the conjunction is always satisfiable.

---

### what the definition leaves out

a space can have many bases. what's not yet clear is whether they all have to be the same
length. they do, and that common length is the **dimension** of $V$.

proving this requires showing that no independent list can be longer than any spanning
list, a consequence of the linear dependence lemma. once you have that, dimension is
well-defined and everything else follows.
