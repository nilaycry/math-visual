so far, every result about linear maps has been basis-free. we proved rank-nullity without ever writing down coordinates. but to compute anything, to actually evaluate $T$ on a vector or find the null space explicitly, you need to pick bases and write things out.

a **matrix** is how you do that. it records everything about a linear map, relative to a choice of bases.

---

### the matrix of a linear map

fix a linear map $T: V \to W$, a basis $v_1, \ldots, v_n$ of $V$, and a basis $w_1, \ldots, w_m$ of $W$.

each $T(v_k)$ is a vector in $W$, so it has a unique representation in the $w$ basis:

$$T(v_k) = A_{1,k}\, w_1 + A_{2,k}\, w_2 + \cdots + A_{m,k}\, w_m$$

before reading the definition, try writing it yourself: you want to record where $T$ sends each basis vector of $V$. you have $n$ basis vectors, each mapping to a vector in $W$ that needs $m$ coordinates. what structure does that suggest?

> **Definition.** Let $T: V \to W$ be a linear map, $v_1, \ldots, v_n$ a basis of $V$, and $w_1, \ldots, w_m$ a basis of $W$. The **matrix of $T$** with respect to these bases, denoted $\mathcal{M}(T)$, is the $m \times n$ matrix whose entry in row $j$, column $k$ is $A_{j,k}$, where
> $$T(v_k) = A_{1,k}\, w_1 + \cdots + A_{m,k}\, w_m$$

column $k$ of $\mathcal{M}(T)$ is the coordinate vector of $T(v_k)$ in the $w$ basis. the columns are the outputs.

this is the thing I wish someone had told me at the start: **a matrix is always a matrix of something, with respect to something.** the same linear map has different matrices in different bases. the matrix isn't the map. it's a description of the map in a language that depends on your choice of coordinates.

---

### a worked example

let $T: \mathbf{F}^3 \to \mathbf{F}^2$ be defined by $T(x, y, z) = (x - y,\, 2z)$.

use the standard bases: $e_1 = (1,0,0)$, $e_2 = (0,1,0)$, $e_3 = (0,0,1)$ for $\mathbf{F}^3$, and $f_1 = (1,0)$, $f_2 = (0,1)$ for $\mathbf{F}^2$.

compute $T$ on each basis vector:

$$T(e_1) = (1, 0) = 1 \cdot f_1 + 0 \cdot f_2$$

$$T(e_2) = (-1, 0) = -1 \cdot f_1 + 0 \cdot f_2$$

$$T(e_3) = (0, 2) = 0 \cdot f_1 + 2 \cdot f_2$$

write these as columns:

$$\mathcal{M}(T) = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$$

to verify: take $v = (3, 1, 4)$, so $T(v) = (3 - 1, 8) = (2, 8)$. multiplying:

$$\begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 1 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 - 1 + 0 \\ 0 + 0 + 8 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 8 \end{pmatrix}$$

the matrix-vector product recovers $T(v)$ in coordinates. I want to linger here, because this is the thing that confused me for longer than I'd like to admit.

the row-column dot product formula is usually taught as a rule. multiply rows by columns and sum. it feels arbitrary. why not some other operation?

here's the real answer: once you decide that the $k$-th column of $\mathcal{M}(T)$ should record $T(v_k)$, the formula for applying $T$ to any vector is forced by linearity. you don't get to choose it.

any $v \in V$ writes uniquely as $v = x_1 v_1 + \cdots + x_n v_n$. because $T$ is linear:

$$T(v) = x_1 T(v_1) + \cdots + x_n T(v_n)$$

so $T(v)$ is a linear combination of the columns of $\mathcal{M}(T)$, with weights $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ given by the coordinates of $v$. the $j$-th coordinate of $T(v)$ is $\sum_k A_{j,k}\, x_k$, which is row $j$ of $A$ dotted with the input.

that's all the formula is. it isn't a convention someone invented. it's the only formula consistent with $T$ being linear and the columns recording what $T$ does to basis vectors.

pause here if this felt fast. the row-column dot product isn't arbitrary. it's a consequence. two things force it: the columns encode $T$ on the basis, and $T$ is linear. if you accept those, the formula writes itself. this same logic is what makes the next result feel inevitable.

---

### matrix multiplication is composition

this is the point of the whole note.

suppose $S: U \to V$ and $T: V \to W$ are linear, with bases chosen for each space. the composition $T \circ S: U \to W$ is also linear, so it also has a matrix. what is it?

$$\mathcal{M}(T \circ S) = \mathcal{M}(T) \cdot \mathcal{M}(S)$$

the formula for matrix multiplication is not an arbitrary rule. it's what you're forced to write down if you want matrix products to correspond to composition.

here's the argument. the $k$-th column of $\mathcal{M}(S)$ records where $S$ sends the $k$-th basis vector of $U$, expressed in the $v$ basis. to find $(T \circ S)(u_k) = T(S(u_k))$, you apply $T$ to that coordinate vector. that application is exactly the matrix-vector product $\mathcal{M}(T) \cdot (\text{column } k \text{ of } \mathcal{M}(S))$. do that for every column of $\mathcal{M}(S)$, and you get all the columns of $\mathcal{M}(T \circ S)$.

the entry formula follows. if $A = \mathcal{M}(T)$ is $m \times n$ and $B = \mathcal{M}(S)$ is $n \times p$, then

$$(AB)_{j,k} = \sum_{r=1}^{n} A_{j,r}\, B_{r,k}$$

this is the dot product of row $j$ of $A$ with column $k$ of $B$. row $j$ of $A$ encodes the $j$-th output coordinate of $T$; column $k$ of $B$ encodes where $S$ sends the $k$-th input. their dot product gives the $j$-th output coordinate of $T \circ S$ on the $k$-th input.

---

### composition does not commute, and neither does multiplication

since $ST$ means "apply $S$ then $T$" and $TS$ means "apply $T$ then $S$," swapping the order gives a different map whenever $S$ and $T$ don't commute. matrix multiplication inherits this: $AB \neq BA$ in general.

often both products aren't even defined at the same time. if $A$ is $m \times n$ and $B$ is $n \times p$, then $AB$ is $m \times p$, but $BA$ requires $B$ to be $p \times m$, which need not hold.

even when both products are defined and the same size, they usually differ. this is one of the places matrix algebra breaks from ordinary algebra. if you find yourself writing $AB = BA$, check whether that's actually true for the specific matrices at hand.

---

### what this doesn't cover yet

the matrix $\mathcal{M}(T)$ depends on the choice of bases. the same linear map looks completely different with different bases for $V$ and $W$. understanding when two matrices represent the same map, and how to convert between representations, is the next topic.

also: we've been describing maps between two different spaces. when $T: V \to V$ is an operator on a single space, you use one basis for both input and output. the theory of those operators, especially when they're diagonalizable, is where the most interesting structure appears.
