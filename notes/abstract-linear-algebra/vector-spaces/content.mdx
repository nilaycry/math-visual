you've worked with vectors in $\mathbb{R}^2$ and $\mathbb{R}^3$ before: arrows in the
plane or in space. you can add two of them and scale one by a number, and those
operations behave nicely. at some point mathematicians noticed that completely
different-looking objects behave exactly the same way.

polynomials like $p(x) = x^2 + 3x + 1$. matrices. continuous functions. arrows in
space. you can add two polynomials and scale a polynomial by a number, and the results
obey the same rules as adding and scaling vectors. same with matrices. same with
functions.

so instead of proving the same theorems over and over for each type of object, you
prove them once: define a **vector space** as any object that supports addition and
scaling satisfying a fixed list of rules. every theorem about vector spaces then
automatically applies to polynomials, matrices, functions, and arrows simultaneously.

that's the whole point. a vector space is an abstraction that captures the essential
behavior of "things you can add and scale."

---

### what you're scaling by: fields

before you can define vector spaces, you need to fix what the scalars are.

in $\mathbb{R}^3$ you scale vectors by real numbers. but what if you wanted to scale by
complex numbers? a **field** is the formal name for a number system where you can add,
subtract, multiply, and divide (except by zero). $\mathbb{R}$, $\mathbb{C}$, and
$\mathbb{Q}$ are all fields.

in this course $\mathbf{F}$ stands for either $\mathbb{R}$ or $\mathbb{C}$, and the
choice is left open so theorems can hold for both. when a result genuinely requires one
or the other, the proof will say so.

---

### the axioms, motivated

now the eight rules. rather than listing them as a table and moving on, here is why
each one is there.

**four rules about addition**

$$u + v = v + u$$

order shouldn't matter. this isn't automatic. you have to require it. (matrix
multiplication, for contrast, doesn't have this property. matrix addition does.)

$$(u + v) + w = u + (v + w)$$

when adding three vectors, grouping shouldn't matter. without this, the expression
$u + v + w$ is ambiguous.

$$\text{there exists } \mathbf{0} \in V \text{ such that } v + \mathbf{0} = v \text{ for all } v$$

there has to be a "do nothing" element. in $\mathbb{R}^2$ it's the origin. for
polynomials it's the zero polynomial. for matrices it's the zero matrix. every
reasonable system has one.

$$\text{for every } v \in V \text{ there exists } w \text{ such that } v + w = \mathbf{0}$$

every vector has an opposite that cancels it. this is what makes subtraction possible:
$u - v$ is defined as $u + (-v)$.

together these four say that $(V, +)$ is an abelian group. that structure comes up
everywhere in mathematics.

**four rules about scalar multiplication**

$$1 \cdot v = v$$

scaling by the field's multiplicative identity should do nothing. without this,
scalar multiplication is disconnected from the field's structure entirely.

$$(ab)v = a(bv)$$

scaling $v$ by $b$ and then by $a$ should give the same result as scaling by $ab$
directly. this says scalar multiplication is compatible with multiplication in
$\mathbf{F}$.

$$a(u + v) = au + av$$

scaling distributes over addition in $V$. this is what makes scalar multiplication
interact coherently with vector addition.

$$(\alpha + \beta)v = \alpha v + \beta v$$

distributing in the other direction: scaling by $\alpha + \beta$ is the same as
scaling by each separately and adding. this connects scalar multiplication to
addition in $\mathbf{F}$.

---

### the formal definition

> **Definition.** A **vector space** over a field $\mathbf{F}$ is a set $V$ together
> with two operations, addition $V \times V \to V$ and scalar multiplication
> $\mathbf{F} \times V \to V$, satisfying the eight axioms above. Elements of $V$
> are called **vectors**. Elements of $\mathbf{F}$ are called **scalars**.

pause here. the definition says nothing about what the elements of $V$ actually are.
they don't have to be tuples or arrows. they just have to support two operations
satisfying these rules. that's the whole abstraction.

---

### why exactly these eight

not too many, not too few.

strong enough that you can build a rich theory: subspaces, bases, dimension, linear
maps, eigenvalues. all of it follows from just these eight rules.

weak enough that many different objects satisfy them, so the theory applies broadly.

if you dropped one axiom, something would break. drop the additive inverse and you
can't subtract. drop associativity of scalar multiplication and scaling becomes
incompatible with field arithmetic. each one is there because a proof somewhere
needs it.

---

### examples

the most important example is $\mathbf{F}^n$: the set of all lists of length $n$ with
entries in $\mathbf{F}$.

$$(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n) \quad \text{where each } x_i \in \mathbf{F}$$

addition and scalar multiplication are defined coordinatewise:

$$(x_1, \ldots, x_n) + (y_1, \ldots, y_n) = (x_1 + y_1, \ldots, x_n + y_n)$$

$$a(x_1, \ldots, x_n) = (ax_1, \ldots, ax_n)$$

the zero vector is $(0, \ldots, 0)$. every axiom holds by checking one coordinate at a
time, reducing each step to the corresponding fact about $\mathbf{F}$. when we write
$(1, 0)$ or $(-3, 2, 1)$ anywhere in these notes, this is the definition we're using.

$\mathbb{R}^n$ satisfies all eight axioms. so do:

- $\mathcal{P}_n(\mathbf{F})$: polynomials of degree at most $n$ with coefficients
  in $\mathbf{F}$, with pointwise addition and scalar multiplication
- $\mathbf{F}^{m \times n}$: all $m \times n$ matrices, with matrix addition and
  scalar multiplication
- $C([0,1])$: continuous functions on $[0,1]$, with pointwise operations

one theorem about vector spaces is a theorem about all of these at once.

note that a plane through the origin in $\mathbb{R}^3$ is a vector space (it
contains the zero vector and is closed under addition and scaling), but a plane that
misses the origin is not: it fails the additive identity axiom since $\mathbf{0}$
isn't in the set.

---

### what the definition leaves out

the axioms say nothing about length, distance, or angle. $\mathbb{R}^n$ has all of
those through the dot product, but a general vector space does not. you can add
vectors and scale them. you cannot measure them until you add more structure.

the structure that adds measurement is an inner product, a separate definition
layered on top of a vector space, not part of what a vector space is.

I think it's worth saying explicitly: a lot of $\mathbb{R}^n$ intuition quietly relies
on the dot product. phrases like "the angle between two vectors" or "the length of a
vector" don't mean anything in a general vector space until you've defined an inner
product. importing that intuition into abstract settings is the most common way to
confuse yourself in this course.
