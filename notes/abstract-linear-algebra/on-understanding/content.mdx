there's a specific feeling that shows up a few weeks into abstract math. you read a proof,
you can check every line, nothing is technically wrong, and yet at the end you have no idea
what just happened. you followed the steps. you didn't understand the proof.

this gap between following and understanding is the central experience of abstract
mathematics, and almost no resource names it directly. if you're hitting it, you're
not missing something obvious. it's what the subject is actually like, and knowing it
exists is most of what you need to navigate it.

---

### what changes when math gets abstract

in a computation-first course, you have anchors. you row-reduce a matrix, you get numbers,
you can verify the answer is right. the answer being right is evidence you understood the
process. in abstract linear algebra, there's no such check. the objects are exact
definitions, the proofs are the only tool, and "verifying a step" means confirming a logical
implication, not getting a number to match.

this sounds harder, and in some ways it is. but it also means the subject becomes more
honest. you can't accidentally get the right answer. if a proof works, it works everywhere,
for any vector space over any field, because the argument only used the definitions.

the flip side: when you don't understand something, you can't paper over it with
computation. the gap is visible immediately, usually as soon as you try a problem.

---

### definitions are load-bearing

in most math you've seen, definitions are just names. "a prime number is..." gives you
a word so you don't have to say "a number divisible only by 1 and itself" every time.
the name is convenient but not the point.

in abstract linear algebra, definitions are almost the whole thing. a vector space is
exactly eight axioms, not a vague notion of "things you can add and scale." a linear
map is exactly two conditions, not the intuition of "something straight." the proofs
work by invoking specific clauses of specific definitions, and if those clauses haven't
landed for you, the proofs will feel like they're coming from nowhere.

I found the clearest test for whether a definition has actually landed is this: can you
produce your own example, one that isn't in the notes? not recall one, but construct one.
if I can't, the definition is sitting in my head as a string of words, not as a real
object I understand. going back and finding a new example almost always reveals the part
I missed.

---

### two kinds of confusion

confusion in abstract math comes in two flavors that feel nearly identical from the
inside but require completely different responses.

the first kind: you're engaged with something genuinely hard. the definition is precise,
the proof is correct, and you're working to see why it's the right way to think about
the problem. this confusion is productive. the understanding is building even when it
doesn't feel like it. the right move is to push forward, do problems, and let the
picture assemble.

the second kind: something earlier didn't fully land. a definition is sitting in your
head as a form of words but not as an object. going forward won't help. every new
proof will feel unmotivated because it's invoking something you don't quite have. the
right move is to go back, find the exact point where it stopped making sense, and
sit there until it does.

the way I tell them apart: in the first kind, I can usually state what I don't see. "I
understand the null space is a subspace, but I don't see why this theorem follows from
that." that's a real confusion about a specific thing. in the second kind, the confusion
is more like fog. I can't point at a specific gap because I don't have enough to know
what's missing.

if it feels like fog, go back.

---

### what it feels like when it's working

I want to be precise about this because "understanding" in abstract math doesn't feel
the way I expected it to.

it's not a sudden clarity. it's more gradual. the objects start to feel real rather than
symbolic. you read a definition and you have a sense of what it's trying to isolate before
you finish reading it. you start to guess what will be true before you see the proof, and
sometimes you're right. proofs feel less like arbitrary sequences of steps and more like
the only reasonable thing to do given what you know.

the clearest signal for me: I can hold the definition in my head as an object and turn it
around, ask what it implies, ask what would break if you removed one of the conditions.
when I can do that, I have it. when I can only recite it, I don't.

there's also a more honest signal: you try a problem cold, without looking at anything,
and you know where to start. not necessarily how to finish. just that you know which
definitions are relevant and which direction to push. that's the state these notes are
trying to get you to.
