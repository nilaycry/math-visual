the eigenvalues note ended with a question: does an operator have enough eigenvectors to form a basis? if it does, the matrix of the operator in that basis is the most transparent form possible. on the diagonal sit the eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n$. off the diagonal: all zeros. the operator acts on each basis direction independently, just scaling it. computing $T^{100}$ or checking invertibility becomes trivial.

not every operator achieves this. the question is what structure we can always guarantee, and when the diagonal form is actually reachable.

---

### diagonalizable operators

before reading the definition, try to write it yourself. what does it mean for the matrix of an operator to be diagonal? it means we found a basis where $T$ scales each vector by some scalar. what is the right name for that?

> **Definition.** An operator $T$ on a finite-dimensional vector space $V$ is **diagonalizable** if $V$ has a basis consisting of eigenvectors of $T$.

if $v_1, \ldots, v_n$ is such a basis, with $Tv_j = \lambda_j v_j$, then the matrix of $T$ with respect to this basis is

$$\mathcal{M}(T) = \begin{pmatrix} \lambda_1 & & 0 \\ & \ddots & \\ 0 & & \lambda_n \end{pmatrix}$$

the $j$-th column records where $v_j$ goes: entirely to $\lambda_j v_j$, so only the $j$-th entry is nonzero.

consider $T: \mathbf{R}^2 \to \mathbf{R}^2$ defined by $T(x, y) = (2x, 3y)$. the standard basis vectors $e_1 = (1, 0)$ and $e_2 = (0, 1)$ satisfy $Te_1 = 2e_1$ and $Te_2 = 3e_2$. they are eigenvectors, and they form a basis. $T$ is diagonalizable, with matrix $\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$.

for a non-example, let $N: \mathcal{P}_2(\mathbf{F}) \to \mathcal{P}_2(\mathbf{F})$ be the differentiation operator $N(p) = p'$. any eigenvector would satisfy $p' = \lambda p$ for some scalar $\lambda$. differentiating a nonzero polynomial strictly lowers its degree. the only polynomial satisfying $p' = 0$ is a constant, and it satisfies $p' = 0 \cdot p$, so $\lambda = 0$ is an eigenvalue. but for $\lambda \neq 0$, no polynomial satisfies $p' = \lambda p$. the eigenspace for $\lambda = 0$ is one-dimensional (the constants), which gives us only one linearly independent eigenvector in a three-dimensional space. $N$ is not diagonalizable.

### distinct eigenvalues force independence

the most useful sufficient condition for diagonalizability is also one of the cleanest results in the subject.

> **Theorem.** Eigenvectors of $T$ corresponding to distinct eigenvalues are linearly independent.

the proof is by induction on the number of distinct eigenvalues. with one eigenvalue and one nonzero eigenvector, linear independence is immediate.

suppose $v_1, \ldots, v_m$ are eigenvectors with distinct eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_m$ and suppose they are linearly dependent. take the shortest dependence relation. since the eigenvectors are nonzero, the relation must involve at least two terms. write it as:

$$a_1 v_1 + a_2 v_2 + \cdots + a_m v_m = 0$$

with all $a_j \neq 0$ and $m$ chosen minimally. apply $T$ to both sides:

$$a_1 \lambda_1 v_1 + a_2 \lambda_2 v_2 + \cdots + a_m \lambda_m v_m = 0$$

subtract $\lambda_m$ times the original relation:

$$a_1(\lambda_1 - \lambda_m) v_1 + \cdots + a_{m-1}(\lambda_{m-1} - \lambda_m) v_{m-1} = 0$$

since the eigenvalues are distinct, $\lambda_j - \lambda_m \neq 0$ for $j < m$, and all $a_j \neq 0$. this is a shorter dependence relation, contradicting minimality.

the practical consequence: if $T$ has $n$ distinct eigenvalues on an $n$-dimensional space, those eigenvectors form a basis, and $T$ is diagonalizable. not every diagonalizable operator has distinct eigenvalues, but distinct eigenvalues always guarantee diagonalizability.

### when diagonalization fails: upper triangular matrices

the differentiation operator cannot be diagonalized. what structure can we always find?

over the complex numbers, the answer is an upper triangular matrix. this is weaker than diagonal, but still useful.

> **Definition.** A matrix is **upper triangular** if all entries below the main diagonal are zero.

$$\begin{pmatrix} \lambda_1 & * & * \\ 0 & \lambda_2 & * \\ 0 & 0 & \lambda_3 \end{pmatrix}$$

the stars represent entries that may or may not be zero.

> **Theorem.** Every operator on a finite-dimensional nonzero complex vector space has an upper triangular matrix with respect to some basis.

the proof is inductive on dimension. in dimension one, any $1 \times 1$ matrix is upper triangular. for dimension $n > 1$: every operator on a finite-dimensional complex vector space has an eigenvalue (we proved this in the previous note). call it $\lambda$, with eigenvector $v_1$.

extend $v_1$ to a basis of $V$, and let $U$ be the span of the remaining $n-1$ vectors. define the operator $\tilde{T}$ on $V / \operatorname{span}(v_1)$: by induction, $\tilde{T}$ has an upper triangular matrix. pull that structure back to choose basis vectors $v_2, \ldots, v_n$ such that $T$ maps the span of $v_1, \ldots, v_k$ into itself for each $k$. in that basis, $T$ is upper triangular.

the diagonal entries of the upper triangular matrix are the eigenvalues of $T$. the matrix doesn't tell us everything, but the diagonal already tells us the eigenvalue data.

---

### invariance and the basis condition

the structural reason upper triangular matrices exist is worth making precise.

> **Theorem.** An operator $T$ has an upper triangular matrix with respect to a basis $v_1, \ldots, v_n$ if and only if $\operatorname{span}(v_1, \ldots, v_k)$ is invariant under $T$ for each $k = 1, \ldots, n$.

the $\Rightarrow$ direction is immediate. if $T$ has an upper triangular matrix, then $Tv_k$ is a linear combination of $v_1, \ldots, v_k$ (no entries below row $k$ in column $k$). so the span of the first $k$ vectors is sent into itself.

for the differentiation example on $\mathcal{P}_2(\mathbf{F})$, use the basis $1, x, x^2$. differentiating sends $x^k$ to $kx^{k-1}$: it never increases degree. so $\operatorname{span}(1)$ is invariant, $\operatorname{span}(1, x)$ is invariant, and $\operatorname{span}(1, x, x^2)$ is the whole space. the matrix of differentiation in this basis is

$$\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 2 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

upper triangular with all zeros on the diagonal. the only eigenvalue is $0$, but the operator is not diagonalizable since the $0$-eigenspace is only one-dimensional.

---

### what this doesn't cover yet

upper triangular matrices exist over $\mathbf{C}$ but not always over $\mathbf{R}$. the rotation operator on $\mathbf{R}^2$ has no real eigenvalues and cannot be put in upper triangular form over the reals. the complex case is special because every polynomial factors completely.

the upper triangular form captures eigenvalues but hides the full action of the operator in the above-diagonal entries. the full structure, including what those entries say about the repeated eigenvalue case, lives in the jordan form, which we return to near the end of the course.

there is also a fundamentally different approach: instead of choosing a clever basis, add geometric structure to the space. with an inner product, certain classes of operators are guaranteed to be diagonalizable. that is what the spectral theorem provides, and it is where the course turns next.
