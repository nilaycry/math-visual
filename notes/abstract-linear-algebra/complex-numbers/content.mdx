Complex numbers appear before vector spaces because the scalars in this course are not
always real numbers. Sometimes the scalars are complex numbers, and that changes which
vector spaces and linear maps behave cleanly.

The first thing to get straight is this: as a set, $\mathbb{C}$ is basically $\mathbb{R}^2$.
A complex number is an ordered pair of real numbers, but written in a way that remembers
which coordinate is supposed to multiply like $i$.

$$
\mathbb{C} = \{a + bi : a,b \in \mathbb{R}\}.
$$

The correspondence is

$$
a + bi \longleftrightarrow (a,b).
$$

So if all you care about is addition, $\mathbb{C}$ behaves exactly like $\mathbb{R}^2$.
The new thing is multiplication.

---

### the definition

The textbook defines addition and multiplication in $\mathbb{C}$ by

$$
(a + bi) + (c + di) = (a + c) + (b + d)i
$$

and

$$
(a + bi)(c + di) = (ac - bd) + (ad + bc)i.
$$

Addition is coordinatewise. Multiplication is the special rule.

You should not memorize the multiplication formula as a random object. It is what happens
if you multiply formally and use $i^2 = -1$:

$$
(a + bi)(c + di) = ac + adi + bci + bd i^2 = (ac - bd) + (ad + bc)i.
$$

That is the whole reason the formula looks the way it does.

---

### what C is adding to R^2

It is correct to think:

$$
\mathbb{C} = \mathbb{R}^2 \text{ with a multiplication rule added.}
$$

But the phrase "with multiplication added" is doing real work.

Ordinary $\mathbb{R}^2$ has vector addition and scalar multiplication by real numbers. It
does not come with a natural way to multiply two vectors and get another vector while also
having division by every nonzero vector.

Complex numbers do. That is why $\mathbb{C}$ is a field, not just a two-dimensional real
vector space.

---

### properties inherited from R

The basic algebraic properties of $\mathbb{C}$ come from the corresponding properties of
$\mathbb{R}$.

For example, to prove multiplication is commutative, write

$$
\alpha = a + bi, \qquad \beta = c + di.
$$

Then

$$
\alpha\beta = (ac - bd) + (ad + bc)i
$$

and

$$
\beta\alpha = (ca - db) + (cb + da)i.
$$

These are equal because multiplication and addition in $\mathbb{R}$ are commutative.

That proof is a template. When the textbook lists the properties of complex arithmetic, the point
is not that they are mysterious new facts. The point is that the definitions of complex
addition and multiplication were engineered so the familiar real-number rules survive.

The properties recorded are:

- commutativity of addition and multiplication
- associativity of addition and multiplication
- additive identity $0$ and multiplicative identity $1$
- additive inverses
- multiplicative inverses for nonzero complex numbers
- distributivity

Those are exactly the kinds of properties a scalar system needs before it can act on vector
spaces.

---

### inverses

The additive inverse is easy:

$$
-(a + bi) = -a - bi.
$$

The multiplicative inverse is the first place where the real and imaginary parts interact.
If $a + bi \neq 0$, then $a$ and $b$ are not both zero, so

$$
a^2 + b^2 > 0.
$$

Then

$$
\frac{1}{a + bi} = \frac{a - bi}{a^2 + b^2}.
$$

The reason is that

$$
(a + bi)(a - bi) = a^2 + b^2.
$$

The number $a - bi$ is called the complex conjugate of $a + bi$. The textbook has not made
conjugation a major object yet, but this inverse formula is already using it.

This is the main proof burden for saying $\mathbb{C}$ is a field: every nonzero complex
number has a multiplicative inverse.

---

### subtraction and division are defined from inverses

The textbook is careful about this.

Subtraction is not a new primitive operation. It is addition of the additive inverse:

$$
\beta - \alpha = \beta + (-\alpha).
$$

Division is not a new primitive operation either. It is multiplication by the multiplicative
inverse:

$$
\frac{\beta}{\alpha} = \beta \cdot \frac{1}{\alpha}, \qquad \alpha \neq 0.
$$

This matters because abstract algebraic structures usually start with a few operations and
define the rest from them. Here the primitive operations are addition and multiplication.
Subtraction and division come afterward.

---

### the notation F

Throughout the text, $\mathbb{F}$ means either $\mathbb{R}$ or $\mathbb{C}$.

Elements of $\mathbb{F}$ are called scalars.

That convention lets one theorem cover both real and complex vector spaces. If a proof only
uses the shared field properties of $\mathbb{R}$ and $\mathbb{C}$, the result can be stated over
$\mathbb{F}$ and avoid proving it twice.

When the difference matters, the text will say so. Later, many spectral results behave more
cleanly over $\mathbb{C}$ because complex polynomials have roots in a way real polynomials
do not always have.

---

### what to remember for vector spaces

For Chapter 1, complex numbers are not mainly about computation. They are about the scalar
system.

The takeaway is:

- $\mathbb{C}$ is $\mathbb{R}^2$ as a set
- addition is coordinatewise
- multiplication is defined so that $i^2 = -1$
- the field properties of $\mathbb{C}$ follow from the field properties of $\mathbb{R}$
- nonzero complex numbers have inverses because $(a + bi)(a - bi) = a^2 + b^2$
- $\mathbb{F}$ means "use either $\mathbb{R}$ or $\mathbb{C}$ as the scalars"

The next definition, vector space, depends on this setup. Before asking what a vector is,
the text first fixes what kind of numbers are allowed to scale it.
