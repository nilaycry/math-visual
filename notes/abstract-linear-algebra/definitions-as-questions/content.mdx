description: "how to read a definition in abstract math, and how you could have arrived at it yourself"

---

definitions in abstract math feel different from definitions elsewhere. they're precise in a way
that can feel arbitrary. why these conditions and not others? why exactly two, or exactly eight?

the antidote is to ask: what question is this definition answering?

every definition in this course is the minimum set of conditions required to capture a specific
idea precisely. the conditions aren't chosen for elegance or tradition. they're the logical
consequence of asking one clear question and not letting yourself stop until you've answered
it exactly.

---

### the question behind a linear map

take the definition of a linear map. a function $T: V \to W$ satisfying $T(u+v) = T(u) + T(v)$ and $T(av) = aT(v)$. those conditions didn't appear from nowhere.

the question behind them: if $V$ and $W$ are vector spaces, what should a "nice" function
between them look like? where "nice" means it respects the structure we've defined.

so: what structure does a vector space have? exactly two operations. addition and scalar
multiplication. that's it. a function respects that structure if and only if it commutes
with both. commutativity with addition gives $T(u+v) = T(u) + T(v)$. commutativity with
scaling gives $T(av) = aT(v)$. two operations, two conditions.

if you'd been asked to define "structure-preserving function between vector spaces" without
seeing the definition first, you would have written exactly that. it's the only reasonable
answer to the question.

---

### the removal test

once you have a definition, a useful habit is to ask whether it's tight. what breaks if you
drop one of the conditions?

for the vector space axioms, try removing commutativity of addition: $u + v = v + u$. you'd
get structures where the order of addition matters, and the proof that $-1 \cdot v = -v$ would
fail. the axiom is there because something specific breaks without it.

for a linear map, try dropping the scaling condition. you'd allow functions that commute with
addition but not with scaling. over the real numbers, such functions exist and are genuinely
pathological: they satisfy $T(u + v) = T(u) + T(v)$ for all $u, v$ but are not linear in any
geometric sense. the scaling condition is what rules them out.

this test, asking what breaks, is more useful than asking what the definition "means" in the
abstract. the conditions mean exactly the things that break when you remove them.

---

### reading definitions this way

when you encounter a definition in these notes, pause before moving on and ask the question
behind it. what is this trying to isolate? what would a naive version of this definition look
like, and why would it be wrong?

for some definitions the answer is immediate. for others, especially the ones that felt
arbitrary to me at first, working out the answer is most of what makes the definition land.
the notes try to do this explicitly. but when they don't, the question is always available.
